# Sortition — Evidence & Constitutional Feasibility Brief

*Apni Sarkar research brief. Drafted June 2026. Non-partisan: this brief assesses institutions and evidence, not parties or politicians.*

**One-line summary.** Selecting ordinary citizens by lottery to deliberate on hard public questions — sortition — has a 40-year evidence base, real constitutional wins (Ireland), real failures (France's implementation gap, Iceland's dead constitution), and a surprisingly clear legal path in India: almost everything worth piloting needs no constitutional amendment at all.

---

## 1. The case for sortition

**What it is.** Sortition means choosing a panel of citizens by stratified random lottery — like a jury, but built to mirror the whole population by age, gender, education, region and (in India's case, necessarily) caste and religion — and giving that panel time, balanced evidence and trained facilitation to reach recommendations on a public question. The modern academic name for these bodies is "mini-publics" or "representative deliberative processes" [1].

**This is no longer a fringe idea.** The OECD's landmark 2020 report *Catching the Deliberative Wave* documented 289 representative deliberative processes commissioned by public authorities between 1986 and October 2019, across all levels of government — 52% local, 30% regional, 15% national [1]. By the 2023 update of the OECD Deliberative Democracy Database, the count had grown to 733 cases (1979–2023), with 160 new processes added between September 2021 and September 2023 alone [2]. Deliberation is now a routinely used governance tool in OECD countries, not an experiment.

**Deliberation measurably changes minds and builds knowledge.** James Fishkin's Deliberative Polling method — poll a random sample, give it balanced briefing materials, moderated small-group discussion and access to competing experts, then poll again — has been run for over three decades by Stanford's Deliberative Democracy Lab [3]. Across deliberative polls in more than 18 countries, participants' policy views changed significantly on roughly 70% of the issues deliberated, with measurable knowledge gains and, on polarised issues, measurable depolarisation [3][5]. Fishkin's *Democracy When the People Are Thinking* (2018) synthesises this record and presents the cross-poll data: ordinary citizens, given good conditions, reason competently about complex policy [5].

**Selection science has matured.** Sortition is no longer "names from a hat". Peer-reviewed selection algorithms published in *Nature* (Flanigan et al., 2021) now produce panels that simultaneously match population quotas and give every volunteer a near-equal chance of selection — these algorithms have been used by dozens of assembly organisers worldwide [6].

**Descriptive representation — a body that looks like the population it serves — the gap sortition fills.** Elected chambers systematically under-represent the median citizen. In India's 18th Lok Sabha (elected 2024): 251 of 543 MPs (46%) declared criminal cases against themselves — the highest ever — including 170 (31%) with serious cases; 93% are crorepatis; and a candidate with declared criminal cases had a 15.3% chance of winning against 4.4% for a candidate with a clean record (ADR analysis of candidate affidavits, 2024) [9] — a gap that partly reflects which candidates major parties choose to field in winnable seats, not simply that criminality wins votes. Only 74 women were elected — 13.6% of the House, down from 14.4% in 2019 (data vintage: June 2024 results) [10].*[Note: Factly and ADR-sourced reports give 74; Business Standard's results analysis gives 73. The percentage is 13.4–13.6% depending on the count; ECI's official constituency-by-constituency data is the authoritative check. The directional finding — a fall from 2019 — is not affected either way.]* A stratified random panel of Indian citizens would, by construction, be roughly half women, overwhelmingly non-crorepati, and proportionally representative of caste, religion, occupation and region. That is the core normative claim of political theorist Hélène Landemore's *Open Democracy* (2020): randomly selected bodies are not a retreat from democracy but a more literal version of it — "rule by the people" rather than rule by a self-selected political class [7].

**Honest framing.** None of this evidence shows that sortition should *replace* elections. The strong evidence is narrower: randomly selected, well-facilitated citizen bodies (a) reach informed, considered judgments, (b) are descriptively representative in ways elected bodies are not, and (c) can break deadlocks on questions elected politicians avoid. What follows separates "evidence shows" from "we propose" throughout.

## 2. What actually happened where it was tried

### Ireland: the benchmark success — with a 2024 caution

Ireland ran two national exercises. The **Convention on the Constitution (established 2012; met 2013–14)** mixed 66 randomly selected citizens with 33 politicians and an independent chair [15]. Its recommendation on marriage equality went to referendum in May 2015 and passed with 62.07% — making Ireland the first country to adopt same-sex marriage by popular vote [16]. The follow-up **Citizens' Assembly (2016–18)** — 99 randomly selected citizens chaired by Supreme Court judge Mary Laffoy — deliberated on the Eighth Amendment (abortion) over five weekends from November 2016 to April 2017; 87% of members concluded the constitutional provision should not be retained in full [12][13]. Parliament put repeal to referendum on 25 May 2018; it passed with 66.4% [14]. Advocates and several academic observers — including Farrell and Suiter's comparative work on Irish citizens' assemblies — credit the assembly with breaking a 35-year political deadlock that elected politicians had refused to touch [48]; though attribution is contested and the counterfactual is unknowable (see Section 6). The 2016–18 Assembly's direct budget was €1,505,960.90 (official procurement figures) [17]; cumulative spending on Ireland's subsequent assemblies has run to over €6.8 million (figures released 2024) [18].

**The caution:** in March 2024, referendums arising (in heavily modified form) from the Citizens' Assembly on Gender Equality were crushed — the "family" amendment lost with 67.7% No, and the "care" amendment with 73.9% No, the largest No vote in Irish referendum history [19]. Lesson: assembly recommendations are not magic legitimacy tokens. When governments redraft assembly proposals into something different and campaign poorly, voters reject them. Sortition's wins in Ireland came when the chain from assembly → parliamentary scrutiny → faithful referendum wording stayed intact.

### France: the Convention Citoyenne pour le Climat — be honest about the gap

150 randomly selected residents deliberated October 2019–June 2020 on cutting emissions 40% "in a spirit of social justice". The French government promised the proposals would go "sans filtre" (unfiltered) to parliament, referendum or direct regulation, and in June 2020 announced acceptance of 146 of the 149 proposals [20]. The process cost €5.4 million, including citizen compensation at jury rates of €86.04/day (official Convention budget) [21]. What followed is the cautionary tale of the field. The resulting Climate and Resilience Law (22 August 2021) did pass [24], but visibly diluted: the citizens' ban on domestic flights where rail alternatives exist under 4 hours became 2.5 hours [24]. The environmental outlet Reporterre counted only about 10% of proposals taken up without modification [22]. (Reporterre's criterion was proposals adopted without modification; another ~37% were modified or partially implemented — see KNOCA assessment [23]; the rest were weakened or dropped.) In their final session (February 2021), the convention's 150 members collectively graded the government's follow-through at 3.3 out of 10 [22]. Lesson: an assembly without a pre-committed, enforceable response mechanism converts citizen effort into disillusionment. The institutional lesson — not the failings of any individual actor — is that a response mechanism must be legislated *before* the body is convened.

### Ostbelgien (Belgium): the first permanent sortition institution

In February 2019 the parliament of Belgium's German-speaking community unanimously adopted a decree creating a **permanent Citizens' Council** whose 24 seats are filled by lot, with members drawn from participants of prior Citizens' Assemblies (the initial cohort comprised 12 randomly selected citizens from the general public, 6 participants drawn from a preceding Citizens' Panel, and 6 party-nominated politicians who were rotated out first; subsequent cohorts draw from prior assembly participants rather than directly from the general public), with one-third of seats replaced every six months [25][27]. The Council sets topics and commissions Citizens' Assemblies of 25–50 people drawn by lot — including 16-year-olds and non-citizen residents — with paid participation [25][27]. Five years in, six assemblies have run, parliament is decree-bound to debate every recommendation with the citizens present, and the Council now actively monitors implementation; evaluators report deliberative norms "slowly but surely" taking root, alongside real weaknesses — early recommendations were too vague to legislate, and parliamentary control of agenda-setting needs watching [26]. Ostbelgien matters because it is the proof-of-concept for *institutionalised* (not one-off) sortition — though in a region of only ~78,000 people.

### Iceland: how a deliberative constitutional process dies

After the 2008 crash, Iceland convened a National Forum of 950 randomly selected citizens, then a 25-member elected Constitutional Council that crowdsourced a draft constitution online. In an October 2012 advisory referendum, two-thirds of voters approved the draft. Parliament then simply never ratified it; the Althing was dissolved in 2013 without a vote, and successor parliaments raised the ratification bar [28][29]. Lesson: a deliberative product with no binding pathway and no elite buy-in can be vetoed by silence. This failure mode — not public rejection, but parliamentary attrition — is the one an Indian national assembly design must engineer against.

### India: a thin but real evidence base

No Indian government has yet commissioned a sortition-based citizens' assembly of the OECD type — we found no verified case in the OECD database or elsewhere (as of June 2026). But three bodies of Indian evidence matter:

1. **Gram sabhas are already a mass deliberative institution.** Sanyal and Rao's *Oral Democracy* (Cambridge, 2018), built on transcripts of nearly 300 gram sabha meetings in South India, calls village assemblies "the largest deliberative institution in human history" [32]. A companion World Bank study of 127 village-assembly transcripts (collected 2003) found these meetings democratically representative in who speaks and what gets discussed [30][31]. Survey work by Besley, Pande and Rao found that landless, scheduled-caste and scheduled-tribe villagers were *more* likely to attend gram sabhas than advantaged groups, and that villages which held gram sabhas were associated with better-targeted BPL cards — an observational association, not a randomised result, since villages that convene gram sabhas differ systematically from those that do not — though women's participation lagged badly [33]. Evidence shows: poor Indians show up and use deliberative forums when the forums decide something real.
2. **Kerala's People's Plan Campaign (1996– )** devolved 35–40% of the state plan budget (₹1,025 crore in the first devolution year, 1997-98) to local governments and routed planning through gram sabhas and development seminars, mobilising millions of participants and generating ~150,000 local projects in the Ninth Plan period [34][35]. The campaign was launched in August 1996; the first tranche of funds was released in the 1997-98 plan year. It is participatory rather than sortition-based, but it is India's strongest proof that structured mass deliberation can steer real budgets at scale — though the literature also records a decline in momentum after the initial campaign phase, uneven project quality across localities, and documented instances of local elite capture, patterns that any sortition design must take seriously [34].
3. **Deliberative Polling has been run with Indian samples.** Stanford's Deliberative Democracy Lab convened a national deliberative forum with 250 Indian participants (alongside 300 US participants) on AI wearables governance [46], and India was one of six low- and middle-income countries in the lab's global Deliberative Poll pilot on antimicrobial resistance with the Trinity Challenge [47]. These demonstrate methodological feasibility — recruitment, stratification, facilitation in Indian languages — though neither was commissioned by an Indian government.

## 3. The strongest objections, taken seriously

**Objection 1: Competence.** *Random citizens lack the expertise to weigh telecom spectrum policy or fiscal federalism.* Full force: a lottery can seat a panel where nobody has read a budget. Best response: the evidence on knowledge gain is the strongest in the field — deliberative polls consistently show large, measurable learning, and participants' views changed on roughly 70% of issues, with measurable knowledge gains [3][5]; researchers attribute the change to information rather than group pressure, though deliberative polls lack control groups, so this attribution is contested (conformity effects, moderator influence, and social desirability are live alternative explanations). Assemblies do not draft statutes; they answer value-laden questions ("should the constitution permit X?") after hearing competing experts — exactly what referendum voters and ministers also do, with less preparation. Concession: competence claims are validated mostly for *single, well-scoped questions* over weeks, not for continuous general-purpose legislating. A standing sortition legislature, as Landemore proposes [7], is far beyond the evidence.

**Objection 2: Accountability and recall.** *Elected representatives can be thrown out; a lottery panel answers to no one.* Full force: this is the single sharpest objection, and it is correct. Best response: accountability should sit where binding power sits — in the advisory model, the panel only recommends, and accountable institutions (legislature, electorate via referendum) decide; the panel's own discipline comes from transparency (published evidence, recorded sessions, OECD-standard independent evaluation) [1]. Concession: this response works *only* for advisory bodies. It is a genuine reason to oppose giving unelected panels binding legislative power, and this brief does not propose that.

**Objection 3: Capture and facilitation bias.** *Whoever picks the experts, writes the briefing papers and moderates the rooms steers the outcome.* Full force: in low-transparency settings this is not hypothetical; a captured secretariat can launder a predetermined answer through a citizen veneer. In India specifically, the gram sabha literature documents how local elites and officials can dominate proceedings [30][32]. Best response: published selection algorithms with verifiable quotas [6]; oversight by a multi-party/all-stakeholder advisory board (the Irish model used opposing campaign groups to vet materials) [13]; full publication of briefing materials and expert rosters; independent evaluation against the OECD's published good-practice principles [1]. Concession: these safeguards raise the cost of capture; they do not eliminate it, and India's weaker state-capacity contexts raise the risk above the European baseline.

**Objection 4: Legitimacy without elections.** *Why should anyone obey people nobody chose?* Full force: political theorist Cristina Lafont's *Democracy without Shortcuts* (2020) argues that asking the public to defer to a mini-public is "blind deference" that bypasses, rather than improves, public deliberation [8]. Best response: in the Irish model the mini-public never replaced the demos — it set an informed agenda and the whole electorate decided by referendum; Lafont herself concedes uses of mini-publics that feed wider public debate rather than substitute for it. Concession: Lafont's critique has real bite against any *binding* sortition chamber, and the March 2024 Irish results show the wider public will not rubber-stamp assembly outputs [19] — which is, in a sense, the system working.

**Objection 5: Scale — 1.4 billion people.** *A 150-person panel "representing" 96.88 crore registered electors [11] is statistically absurd; India's diversity (22 scheduled languages, thousands of jatis) cannot fit in a room.* Full force: a 150-member national panel cannot contain even one member from most language-by-caste-by-region cells; claims of mirror representation are weaker in India than anywhere they have been made. Best response: stratified sampling delivers proportionality on the *major* axes (gender, rural/urban, religion, broad caste category, state cluster, income) — the same axes on which the Lok Sabha is most skewed [9][10]; and the federal design answer is to deliberate at the level where the polity is small enough — panchayat, district, state — with the national tier reserved for genuinely national questions, fed by state-level assemblies. Concession: any single national assembly's composition will be contestable, and adversaries will contest it; oversampling and multiple parallel panels mitigate but do not resolve this.

**Objection 6: The implementation gap (the France problem).** *Governments will use assemblies as participation-washing, then shelve the output.* Full force: France accepted "146 of 149" proposals rhetorically and delivered a fraction [20][22][23]; Iceland's parliament killed a referendum-approved draft by inaction [28]. This is the most common way these processes fail, not the rare edge case. Best response: legislate the response mechanism first — Ostbelgien's decree obliges parliament to debate every recommendation with citizens present and to justify rejections [25][26]; tie assemblies to decisions with built-in execution paths (a referendum, a budget line, a statutory deadline). Concession: no design can force a determined government to act; sortition shifts agendas and burden of justification, it does not abolish politics.

## 4. The Indian constitutional path

This is the core finding: **every step of a credible Indian sortition programme up to and including a national advisory citizens' assembly requires no constitutional amendment.** Only a body with *binding legislative power* would require amendment — and that path is constitutionally hazardous and not recommended.

### 4.1 The gram sabha is an existing direct-democracy organ

Article 40 (Directive Principles) directs the State to organise village panchayats as units of self-government. The 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendment Acts (1992; assented 20 April 1993 after ratification by state legislatures) inserted Part IX (Articles 243–243O, panchayats) and Part IXA (Articles 243P–243ZG, municipalities), plus the Eleventh and Twelfth Schedules [36]. Article 243A states in full: *"A Gram Sabha may exercise such powers and perform such functions at the village level as the Legislature of a State may, by law, provide"* [37]. Article 243G similarly lets state legislatures endow panchayats with self-government powers; Article 243W does the same for municipalities, including ward committees under Article 243S [36].

The legal consequence is decisive: **the design of village-level deliberation is already a plenary state-legislative subject** (meaning the state legislature can pass any law on this topic it chooses, without central permission). A state law (or, for pilots, executive rules under an existing state Panchayati Raj Act) can create a randomly selected "deliberative panel of the gram sabha" — a stratified sample of the village electoral roll that meets, deliberates with facilitation, and places reasoned recommendations before the full gram sabha and the panchayat's planning process. This is constitutionally indistinguishable from the working groups and neighbourhood committees Kerala created for the People's Plan Campaign [34][35]. India currently has roughly 2.6 lakh panchayats with over 31 lakh elected representatives, about 46% of them women [43][44] — the institutional substrate exists everywhere; what is missing is deliberative design. The Ministry of Panchayati Raj's own Devolution Index 2024 (with IIPA) puts the national average at 43.9 out of 100 (up from 39.9 in 2013-14), with Karnataka highest at 72.2 [42] — devolution is real, and barely half-done.

### 4.2 State-level citizens' assemblies: ordinary state statute

States routinely create advisory and even statutory bodies — state planning boards, state finance commissions, public service commissions — by statute or executive order. Nothing in the Constitution restricts a state legislature from creating, by ordinary law, a **State Citizens' Assembly**: a stratified random sample of the state's electoral rolls, convened for a defined question, whose report the government must table in the Assembly with a reasoned response within a fixed period. State executive power under Article 162 is coextensive with state legislative power (a state cabinet can do by executive order anything its legislature could do by law), so even a cabinet order suffices for pilots; a statute is preferable for permanence and for guaranteeing the response obligation. No constitutional provision is touched: the body advises; the legislature and government decide.

### 4.3 National advisory citizens' assembly: parliamentary statute or executive resolution

Two proven instruments exist:

- **Executive resolution.** NITI Aayog itself was created by a Cabinet Secretariat resolution dated 1 January 2015 — no statute, no amendment [41]. A "National Citizens' Assembly" attached to NITI Aayog or the Ministry of Panchayati Raj could be created the same way tomorrow. (Weakness: what a resolution creates, a resolution can abolish — the Iceland failure mode [28].)
- **Parliamentary statute.** Parliament has created advisory and quasi-independent bodies by ordinary law (National Human Rights Commission via the Protection of Human Rights Act 1993; National Commission for Women Act 1990). An ordinary Act of Parliament — simple majority — can establish a permanent national citizens' assembly system, fix its selection method, fund it, and bind the government to respond to its reports within, say, six months, with reports laid before both Houses and referred to the relevant Department-related Standing Committee.

Neither route amends the Constitution because neither touches Parliament's composition (Article 79) or legislative procedure. The assembly recommends; Parliament disposes.

### 4.5 The selection-criteria question: stratification and equality-clause scrutiny

The statement above — "advisory sortition requires no constitutional amendment" — is accurate as far as it goes, but the design of selection quotas raises a distinct question. If a state or national assembly uses statistical stratification by caste and religion to mirror the population, a petitioner could argue that state-mandated classification on those grounds violates Articles 14 (equality before law), 15 (prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, caste, sex) and 16 (equal opportunity in public employment) — the same nerves touched by reservation jurisprudence, even though the mechanism is different.

The strongest distinguishing argument is that sortition stratification is not preferential treatment of a group but statistical representativeness: every individual is assigned to a cell and has a near-equal chance of selection within it (the Flanigan et al. algorithm [6] was designed precisely to make individual selection probabilities as equal as possible while meeting group quotas). Courts have, in analogous contexts, permitted classification for descriptive purposes that they would not permit for benefits allocation. But this distinction has never been tested in an Indian court, and a stratification design that includes religion — constitutionally among the most sensitive axes in India — would invite a writ petition on day one.

Practical bottom line: **advisory sortition requires no constitutional amendment, but the design of selection quotas must be engineered to survive equality-clause scrutiny — an open legal question that requires practitioner-reviewed opinion before any pilot launches.** The selection methodology should be published and pre-cleared with the state's law department against Articles 14–16 before the assembly is convened.

### 4.4 What WOULD require constitutional amendment — and why we advise against it

Giving any sortition body **binding legislative power** — a third chamber, a citizen veto, or replacement of either House — would require amending Part V of the Constitution (Articles 79–122) under **Article 368**. Article 368(2) requires a special majority in each House (majority of total membership plus two-thirds of those present and voting); its proviso additionally requires **ratification by the legislatures of not less than one-half of the states** for amendments touching, among other things, Articles 54–55 (presidential election), Article 73/162 (executive power), Chapter IV of Part V and Chapter V of Part VI (judiciary), Chapter I of Part XI (Centre–state legislative relations), the Seventh Schedule lists, "the representation of the States in Parliament", and Article 368 itself [38]. A citizen chamber sharing legislative power would squarely affect the representation of states in Parliament and Centre–state legislative relations, triggering state ratification.

Beyond procedure stands the **basic structure doctrine**. In *Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala* (1973), a 13-judge bench held 7–6 that Parliament's amending power under Article 368 cannot destroy the Constitution's basic structure — which the Court's jurisprudence has held to include democracy, free and fair elections, federalism and judicial review [39]. In *Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain* (1975), the Supreme Court struck down clauses (4) and (5) of Article 329A inserted by the 39th Amendment — clause (4) wiped out the pre-existing Allahabad High Court judgment against the Prime Minister's election; clause (5) barred courts from hearing any future challenge to specified elections — precisely because **free and fair elections are part of the basic structure** — the first time an amendment was invalidated on basic-structure grounds [40]. An amendment replacing or overriding elected representation with selection by lot would face a serious, plausibly fatal, basic-structure challenge: a court could readily hold that "democracy" as constitutionalised in India means *electoral* democracy. (A theorist might answer that sortition is also democracy — Athens thought elections were the oligarchic option — but no Indian court has ever been asked to, and a 5-year reform programme should not hang on winning that argument.) Honest bottom line: **advisory sortition requires no constitutional amendment, but the design of selection quotas must be engineered to survive equality-clause scrutiny — an open legal question (see Section 4.5 above); binding sortition is procedurally heavy and constitutionally uncertain at best.** That is why the pathway below is advisory by design.

## 5. A credible 5-phase pathway

**Phase 1 (Years 1–2): Randomly selected deliberative panels in 500–1,000 gram panchayats and 20–30 urban wards, in 3–4 willing states.**
*Legal instrument:* state government order or rules under the existing state Panchayati Raj / Municipalities Act; statutory amendment optional (Articles 243A/243G/243W head-room) [37][36]. *Who must act:* state Panchayati Raj departments; State Election Commissions or panchayat secretariats supply electoral-roll sampling frames; an independent academic consortium runs selection per published algorithms [6]. *Precedent:* Kerala People's Plan working groups and grama sabha task forces [34][35]; gram sabha deliberation evidence [30][32][33]. *Success criteria:* panels statistically match roll demographics (published audit); attendance ≥70% across sessions (benchmarked against OECD-documented assembly retention averages [1]; typical gram sabha quorum attendance is frequently below 20% of the electorate [33], making sortition-panel retention a distinct and measurable improvement); ≥50% of panel recommendations addressed in the Gram Panchayat Development Plan with written reasons, verified by an independent academic evaluator named before the pilot begins; independent evaluation against OECD guidelines [1]. Numeric targets are negotiable placeholders benchmarked against the comparators cited; they should be finalised in consultation with the state Panchayati Raj department before the pilot launches. *Timeline:* design 6 months, two annual GPDP cycles.

**Phase 2 (Years 2–3): District and city citizens' juries on a concrete allocative question (e.g., untied-fund priorities, waste, mobility).**
*Legal instrument:* municipal corporation resolution / district planning committee (Article 243ZD) decision; state order. *Who:* 8–10 districts across pilot states; ward committees under Article 243S as the anchor in cities [36]. *Precedent:* the OECD database's 52% local share of all deliberative processes [1]. *Success criteria:* juries of 30–50, three-plus deliberation days; government written response within 90 days; baseline-vs-endline participant knowledge surveys replicate international knowledge-gain findings [3][5] in Indian conditions. *Timeline:* two budget cycles.

**Phase 3 (Years 3–4): First State Citizens' Assembly (100–150 members, one named question).**
*Legal instrument:* state statute creating the assembly, the response obligation, and member protections (paid leave, per-diems at jury-equivalent rates as in France [21]). *Who:* states scoring highest on the Ministry of Panchayati Raj's own Devolution Index 2024 [42] — a central-government publication — and with cross-party floor support; pilot selection should be capacity-based, not aligned with any party configuration. Chaired by a retired High Court or Supreme Court judge, on the Irish model [13]. *Precedent:* Ireland 2016–18 (99 members, five weekends, €1.5 million) [12][17]. *Success criteria:* recommendations tabled in the Vidhan Sabha with a government response within six months; media reach measured; selection methodology published in advance, pre-cleared by the state's law department against Articles 14–16, with the full audit trail released for independent verification (treat any litigation outcome as information rather than a pass/fail gate — a pending writ petition could outlast the assembly's entire lifespan). *Timeline:* 12–15 months end-to-end.

**Phase 4 (Years 4–5): National Advisory Citizens' Assembly.**
*Legal instrument:* preferably an ordinary Act of Parliament; minimally a Cabinet resolution on the NITI Aayog precedent [41]. *Who:* Government of India (Ministry of Panchayati Raj or NITI Aayog as host); selection by an independent statutory secretariat from ECI electoral rolls (the largest verified national sampling frame available — 96.88 crore electors as of January 2024 [11] — whose known coverage gaps, under-18 exclusions, and accuracy disputes the selection audit must document and correct for); multilingual facilitation building on the Stanford India deliberative forums' methods [46][47]. *Topic discipline:* one national question with referendum-or-statute execution potential. *Precedent:* Ireland's parliament-resolution model [12]; Ostbelgien's mandatory parliamentary debate of recommendations [25][26]. *Success criteria:* report laid before both Houses; standing-committee examination; government reasoned response within six months — the anti-France clause [22]. *Timeline:* statute in year 4, first assembly seated by year 5.

**Phase 5 (Beyond year 5): Entrenchment debate — permanence, not power.**
*Legal instrument:* statutory permanence (an Ostbelgien-style standing Citizens' Council that sets topics and monitors follow-up [25]); constitutional amendment **only** if India later chooses to give assemblies any binding trigger (e.g., a right to force a parliamentary vote), which would invoke Article 368's special majority, likely state ratification, and basic-structure review [38][39][40]. *Who:* Parliament, states, and — properly — a national public argument. *Success criteria for even starting this debate:* phases 1–4 evaluated positively by independent researchers; cross-party sponsorship; public awareness above a measured threshold. We propose treating Phase 5 as an open question, not a goal.

**Indicative five-year cost.** Comparator costs, converted at an indicative ₹90–100 per euro (mid-2026 order of magnitude; conversion is ours, flagged as an assumption): Ireland's 99-member, 18-month assembly: €1.5 million ≈ ₹14–15 crore [17]; France's 150-member convention: €5.4 million ≈ ₹49–54 crore [21]. Assuming Indian delivery costs at or below European levels (lower venue/per-diem costs, offset by translation needs — an assumption, not a sourced fact), the full five-year programme — Phase 1–2 pilots (~₹40–80 crore including evaluation), two state assemblies (~₹30–60 crore), one national assembly plus standing secretariat (~₹60–110 crore) — totals roughly **₹130–250 crore over five years**. Note: the single largest India-specific delivery constraint not captured in these comparators is the facilitation workforce. Phase 1 alone needs trained, multilingual facilitators for 500–1,000 panels; building that pipeline (likely through SIRDs and an academic consortium) carries a training cost that sits inside the Phase 1–2 envelope but is not independently sourced — it is flagged here as an uncosted assumption like the currency conversion. For scale: the Centre for Media Studies estimated total expenditure around the 2024 Lok Sabha election at ₹1.35 lakh crore [45]; the entire programme is about 0.1–0.2% of one election cycle's estimated spending, and far below 0.001% of annual GDP in any year. These programme totals are bottom-up estimates from cited comparators, not budgeted figures; reliable India-specific cost data does not yet exist.

## 6. What we don't know / where we could be wrong

**Thin evidence on long-run institutionalisation.** Only Ostbelgien (pop. ~78,000, five years, six assemblies) tests *permanent* sortition [25][26]. Everything else is one-off. Whether standing citizen bodies stay healthy for decades — or ossify, professionalise, or get captured — is unknown everywhere, not just in India.

**No Indian government-commissioned citizens' assembly exists to learn from.** Our India evidence is gram sabha deliberation studies [30][32][33], Kerala's participatory planning [34][35], and two foreign-run deliberative forums with Indian samples [46][47]. The leap from "villagers deliberate well in assemblies that allocate BPL cards" to "a stratified state-wide panel deliberates well on policy" is plausible but unproven. Phase 1 exists precisely to test it; it may fail.

**Elite capture in low-state-capacity settings.** European safeguard stacks assume functioning procurement, free local media and independent academia at every site. The same gram sabha literature we cite for deliberative competence also documents officials and local elites dominating meetings [30][32]. Sortition panels could become one more institution that *pradhan patis* (husbands who govern through their elected wives) and contractors work around — or through. We do not know the capture rate, and no one does.

**Representation claims will be contested — sometimes correctly.** Stratification on caste and religion is normatively and legally delicate (quota design within a random sample touches the same nerves as reservation policy). A panel that visibly under-represents any group will be attacked, and in a 150-person panel some group always will be. We could be wrong that this is manageable.

**The Fishkin numbers travel imperfectly.** The ~70%-of-issues opinion-change figure aggregates heterogeneous polls, mostly with literate samples and written briefing materials [3][5]. Oral-first, multilingual deliberation at Indian scale may show different dynamics; the *Oral Democracy* findings are encouraging but are about a different institution [32].

**The counterfactual problem.** Ireland's referendums might have passed anyway on generational change; attributing 66.4% [14] to the assembly is an inference, not an experiment. The 2024 Irish defeats [19] cut both ways: they show assemblies don't manufacture consent, which is reassuring for democrats and sobering for advocates.

**The political economy of adoption — who loses and why anyone would adopt this.** The pathway never names who loses power and will therefore resist: MLAs, MPs, sarpanch networks and party machines whose agenda-setting monopoly citizen assemblies dilute. "Willing states" (Phase 1) and "cross-party floor support" (Phase 3) are assumed into existence without explaining why any incumbent would build a rival source of legitimacy — the Iceland lesson (elite veto by silence [28]) applied to India's much denser machine-politics environment. The honest answer is that elected representatives have rational reasons to adopt sortition *in specific circumstances*: assemblies give political cover for unpopular but necessary decisions and spread blame on deadlocked questions — the actual Irish motivation for the abortion and marriage-equality assemblies was precisely that no party wanted to own the outcome. Sequencing should start where assemblies relieve rather than threaten incumbents: local allocative questions where the panchayat benefits from documented citizen support, not constitutional questions where a national assembly threatens established hierarchies. The resistance from each tier is predictable and should be designed around, not wished away.

**Topic choice can weaponize the institution.** The riskiest unaddressed scenario is that the question put to an assembly is itself the failure mode. Ireland's successes were on morally charged but politically "hot potato" questions where all parties wanted cover. The obvious Indian analogues — Uniform Civil Code, conversion laws, delimitation, language policy — could communalize an assembly or let a government launder a majoritarian answer through a "citizen" veneer (Objection 3's capture concern applied to topic choice rather than facilitation). Early phases should be restricted to allocative and administrative questions — budgets, services, infrastructure — where the answer is unlikely to split the assembly along identity lines. Identity-charged constitutional questions should only be considered, if ever, after the institution has earned cross-community trust over multiple rounds, with topic-setting power held by a multi-party body on the Ostbelgien Council model [25].

**Pilot success may not transfer.** Piloting in willing, high-devolution states (Kerala, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu) means even a clean Phase 1–3 success says relatively little about Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, or other low-capacity states where most Indians live — yet Phase 4 scales nationally on that evidence. This selection bias is baked into the design: states self-select for capacity and willingness. At least one pilot cluster in a below-median Devolution Index state should be required before Phase 4 proceeds, to test whether the model transfers or merely validates the already-favourable conditions.

**Where the strongest counterargument stands.** Lafont's blind-deference critique [8] and the accountability objection are not fully answered by "it's only advisory": even advisory bodies consume agenda space and legitimacy. If phases 1–3 produce shelved reports and cynical participants — the France outcome [22] — the honest conclusion would be that India's marginal reform rupee is better spent deepening the deliberative institutions it already has: the 2.6 lakh panchayats and their gram sabhas [43][37].
