# Education — Evidence Brief

**School education and foundational skills | Apni Sarkar | June 2026**

India has nearly solved the problem of getting children into school. It has not solved the problem of what happens once they are there. Enrolment is near-universal at the elementary level, dropout rates are falling, and the teacher workforce crossed one crore for the first time in 2024-25 [2]. Yet fewer than one in four rural government-school children in Std III can read a Std II-level text [1], and the government's own national assessment finds Grade 9 students averaging a mathematics score of just 37% — fewer than four in ten questions answered correctly [4]. This brief lays out where India stands, why, what has demonstrably worked, and a costed five-year plan.

A note on method: every number below carries its survey year or budget year. Where we propose something, we say "we propose" — and we flag the evidence gaps in Section 6.

---

## 1. Where India stands

**1. Foundational reading is improving but remains low.** In ASER 2024 (rural household survey, fieldwork late 2024; 6.49 lakh children in 17,997 villages across 605 districts), 23.4% of Std III children in government schools could read a Std II-level text — up from 16.3% in 2022 and above the pre-COVID 20.9% of 2018, but still meaning roughly three in four cannot [1][5].

**2. Foundational arithmetic shows the same pattern.** 33.7% of rural Std III children could do a two-digit subtraction with borrowing in 2024, up from 25.9% in 2022 [1][5]. In Std V, 30.7% could do simple division (2024), versus 25.6% in 2022 and 27.9% in 2018 [1][6]. Among Std VIII children, only 45.8% could divide (2024) — essentially flat since 2018 (44.1%) [6].

**3. Learning poverty is the headline summary.** The World Bank's India Learning Poverty Brief (April 2024 update, based on the latest pre-pandemic data) estimates that 56% of Indian children at late-primary age cannot read and understand a simple age-appropriate text [7].

**4. The state's own assessment shows low performance at every tested grade.** PARAKH Rashtriya Sarvekshan 2024 (the successor to NAS; conducted 4 December 2024; ~21.15 lakh students in Classes 3, 6 and 9 across 74,229 schools) found Grade 3 students answering roughly 50–67% of language and mathematics items correctly, Grade 6 mathematics competencies ranging roughly 29–42%, and Grade 9 mathematics averaging 37% — the lowest score of any grade and subject — with the weakest Grade 9 competencies (fractions, applying percentages, number sets) at only 28–31% of items correct [4][8]. One caution on reading these numbers: each grade was tested on its own grade-referenced items, so percent-correct figures are not directly comparable across grades.

**5. Enrolment is high; retention to secondary is the cliff.** UDISE+ 2024-25 records total enrolment of 24.69 crore (down ~11 lakh from 24.80 crore in 2023-24) [2][3]. The Gross Enrolment Ratio (enrolment at a stage as a share of the relevant age cohort, counting over- and under-age students) is 90.3% at middle level, 68.5% at secondary and 58.4% at higher secondary; the secondary Net Enrolment Ratio — the share of secondary-age children actually enrolled at secondary level — is just 47.5% (boys 46.6%, girls 48.6%) [2][9][46].

**6. Dropout is falling but still bites at secondary.** Secondary-level dropout fell from 10.9% (2022-23) to 8.2% (2024-25); the middle-to-secondary transition rate is 86.6% [2]. ASER 2024 finds only 7.9% of rural 15–16-year-olds not enrolled, down from 13.1% in 2018 [10].

**7. Teachers: more than ever, but badly distributed.** The teacher workforce crossed 1 crore in 2024-25 (up 6.7% on 2022-23), and average pupil-teacher ratios (10–21 across stages) are comfortably within the NEP 2020 norm of 30 [2]. Yet the Parliamentary Standing Committee on Education (Report No. 368, August 2025) flags roughly 10 lakh vacant teaching posts, with secondary-level vacancies rising from 2.34 lakh to over 4.09 lakh between 2023-24 and 2024-25, and recommends ending reliance on contractual hiring [11]. About 1.04 lakh schools still run with a single teacher (UDISE+ 2024-25) [12].

**8. Teacher absence is a measured, costed governance failure — last measured a decade and a half ago.** The last nationally representative measurement (unannounced visits to schools in 1,297 villages; fieldwork c. 2010, published 2017) found 23.6% of teachers absent on any given day, at an estimated fiscal cost of ~US$1.5 billion per year; villages with regular inspections had absence rates 6.5 percentage points lower — an association in observational data, not an experimental result [13]. No comparable re-survey exists; that measurement gap is itself a finding (see Intervention 4).

**9. Early childhood enrolment is rising; quality is unmeasured at scale.** 77.4% of rural 3-year-olds were enrolled in some pre-primary institution in 2024, up from 68.1% in 2018; anganwadis remain the largest provider, enrolling over half of all 3- and 4-year-olds [14]. The Saksham Anganwadi & POSHAN 2.0 umbrella got ₹23,100 crore in Budget 2026-27 (against a budget estimate of ₹21,960 crore and a revised estimate of ₹20,949 crore in 2025-26) [15]; anganwadi workers' central honorarium has been static at ₹4,500/month since its last revision in October 2018, when it rose from ₹3,000 [16][44].

**10. The digital divide is real but narrowing.** ASER 2024: ~90% of rural 14–16-year-olds live in a household with a smartphone and 82.2% can use one, but only 57% used it for any educational activity in the reference week (76% used it for social media); 36.2% of boys versus 26.9% of girls own a phone [17].

**11. Public spending is well below the NEP target.** Combined Centre-plus-states public expenditure on education has hovered around 4–4.6% of GDP in recent years (Ministry of Education, Analysis of Budgeted Expenditure on Education; World Bank indicator series) [18][19] — against the 6% goal restated by NEP 2020. In Budget 2026-27 the Education Ministry received ₹1.39 lakh crore (+8.27% year-on-year), of which the Department of School Education & Literacy got ₹83,562 crore, Samagra Shiksha ₹42,100 crore, PM POSHAN ₹12,750 crore and PM SHRI ₹7,500 crore [20][21][22].

**12. The policy deadline is now.** NIPUN Bharat (launched July 2021) committed India to universal foundational literacy and numeracy by the end of Grade 3 by 2026-27 — i.e., this coming academic year [23]. On ASER 2024 numbers, the country will miss it by a wide margin [1].

## 2. Root causes

**Constraint 1: The curriculum outpaces the child.** Indian syllabi assume grade-level proficiency that most children do not have; teachers are obliged to "complete the syllabus" rather than teach where children actually are. This is the central diagnosis behind the Teaching at the Right Level (TaRL) literature: six randomized evaluations across seven states found that regrouping children by learning level, not grade, produces some of the largest effect sizes in education research — up to 0.70 standard deviations in language in UP camps (in plain terms, roughly enough to move a mid-ranked child into the top quarter of the class) — precisely because default instruction misses most of the class [24][25]. PARAKH 2024 shows low performance at every tested grade, with older students further from their own grade-level expectations — a pattern consistent with, though not proof of, accumulating curriculum mismatch [4]. (Because each grade is tested on its own grade-referenced items, cross-grade percent-correct figures cannot be read as a child's trajectory through school.)

**Constraint 2: Weak accountability for learning, strong accountability for inputs.** The system measures and rewards enrolment, infrastructure and scheme delivery (which have improved [2]) rather than learning. Teacher absence of 23.6% persisted despite a decade of large input investments; the same study found monitoring frequency — not salary levels — was the strongest correlate of presence [13]. Until 2021 India had no regular census-based learning assessment cycle; PARAKH is new and its results are not yet tied to any consequence for districts or states [4].

**Constraint 3: Teacher posting and vacancy concentration, not aggregate shortage.** With over 1 crore teachers and a national PTR of 10–21 [2], India's problem is distribution: ~10 lakh vacancies concentrated in a handful of states [11], 1.04 lakh single-teacher schools [12], and surging secondary-level vacancies just as enrolment pressure shifts to secondary [11].

**Constraint 4: The foundation is laid before Grade 1, and that stage is the weakest-funded.** GEEAP (the World Bank/FCDO/UNICEF/USAID expert panel) rates early childhood education among the most cost-effective "great buys" in global education [26]. India's main ECCE provider is the anganwadi system, whose workers are paid a ₹4,500/month central honorarium and carry nutrition, health and pre-school duties simultaneously [16]; ECCE quality is not measured at scale in any official series.

**Constraint 5: Secondary schooling is where poor foundations, costs and distance compound.** Secondary NER of 47.5% and an 8.2% dropout rate [2][9] reflect children who arrive at Grade 9 unable to read fluently (Section 1) and face higher direct and opportunity costs. The gender gap in device access [17] and the static Std VIII learning numbers [6] suggest the pipeline into secondary is leaky in quality terms even where enrolment holds.

**Constraint 6: Money is below target, but the bigger gap is allocative.** At ~4–4.6% of GDP [18][19], India spends less than the NEP 6% benchmark — but the absence-cost evidence [13] and the contrast between input growth and flat Std VIII outcomes [6] imply that *how* money is spent (measurement, remediation, ECCE, teacher deployment) binds at least as tightly as how much.

## 3. What has worked

### International

**Vietnam — middle-income country, OECD-level learning.** In PISA 2022, Vietnamese 15-year-olds scored 469 in mathematics against an OECD average of 472, with 72% reaching baseline proficiency (OECD average 69%) — at a GDP per capita of roughly US$4,100 (2022) [27][28]. The World Bank's analysis credits high minimum teacher standards (with low absenteeism), near-universal primary and lower-secondary completion, and sustained, focused public investment under the 2005 Education Law [29]. *Transferability caveat:* careful econometric work finds observable school and household factors explain at most ~30% of Vietnam's outperformance [30] — culture, exam selection and system coherence matter, and India cannot simply copy inputs. The replicable core is teacher professional standards plus relentless focus on universal basics.

**Sobral, Brazil — a measured municipal turnaround.** Sobral, a poor municipality in Ceará state, ranked 1,366th of Brazil's 5,570 municipalities on the national IDEB education index in 2005; by 2017 it ranked first in both primary and lower-secondary education [31][32]. The documented recipe: assess every child's reading level twice a year and use results formatively; give teachers structured lesson plans, aligned materials and continuous coaching; appoint head teachers on merit; and (at state level) Ceará tied a share of inter-municipal tax transfers to learning results [31][33]. *Transferability caveat:* Sobral is one municipality of ~200,000 people with two decades of political continuity; the relevant Indian analogue is the district, and the incentive design (Ceará's results-linked transfers) is the most portable piece.

### Indian states

**Tamil Nadu — Illam Thedi Kalvi (2021–24), the best-evidenced post-COVID recovery at scale.** A panel study of ~19,000 rural Tamil Nadu children (Singh, Romero & Muralidharan; NBER WP 30552, 2022) measured COVID learning losses of ~0.70 SD in maths and ~0.34 SD in language by December 2021, then found two-thirds of the deficit recovered within six months of school reopening — with the state's volunteer-run after-school remediation programme, Illam Thedi Kalvi (ITK), accounting for ~24% of cohort-level recovery [34]. ITK deployed ~1.65–1.8 lakh village volunteers, reached ~96 lakh children over its run, and cost ₹660.35 crore across 2021-22 to 2024-25 [35] — on the order of a few hundred rupees per child per year. The recovery was progressive: weaker students gained more [34].

**Delhi — government-school reform with improved proximate outcomes (interpret with care).** Independent reviews (Brookings, 2023; Education Next) document the 2015–20 reform package — infrastructure, school leadership empowerment, the "Mission Buniyaad" foundational-skills push, and teacher development — alongside rising Class 12 pass rates (88% in 2014-15 to 97% in 2023-24) and reported jumps in grade-level reading and numeracy in grades 6–9 during 2018-19 [36][37]. *Honesty caveat:* there is no randomized or quasi-experimental evaluation; board pass rates are partly an artefact of who is allowed to sit the exam, and the strongest verified gains are from programme-administered assessments. We treat Delhi as suggestive evidence that visible state commitment plus foundational-skills missions can shift a large urban system — not as proof of a specific causal package.

**Promising but unattributed: state-level ASER surges.** Uttar Pradesh and Punjab posted some of the largest state-level ASER gains in 2024 — UP's government-school Std III reading roughly doubled from its 2018 level — coinciding with intensive NIPUN-branded FLN implementation [1][5]. These gains have not been independently decomposed into NIPUN effects versus post-COVID recovery effects (see Section 6.5), so we list them here as a descriptive trend, not as attributed impact.

**The underlying pedagogy, by contrast, is proven.** J-PAL's six RCTs of Pratham's TaRL across seven states (2001–2016) consistently produced gains of 0.07–0.70 SD when the model was implemented faithfully, with government-volunteer "camps" the strongest variant [24][25]. GEEAP's 2023 Smart Buys review — synthesizing 13,000+ studies — ranks structured pedagogy (lesson plans + materials + coaching) and TaRL-style targeted instruction among the few "great buys" in global education, and rates hardware-only technology distribution among the bad buys [26].

## 4. The 5-year plan (2026-27 → 2030-31)

> Evidence shows the binding constraints; the following packages are what **we propose**. Costs are indicative, with the cost basis cited; all targets name their baseline and measurement instrument.

### Intervention 1 — A second-generation FLN mission: NIPUN with Teaching at the Right Level built in

- **Problem:** 76.6% of rural government-school Std III children cannot read a Std II text (ASER 2024) [1]; NIPUN Bharat's 2026-27 deadline will be missed [23].
- **Intervention:** Reset NIPUN with a 2030-31 horizon and make intensive learning camps the primary engine — ITK-style, volunteer-and-teacher-run camps for the lowest-performing third of children in Grades 3–8 — reinforced by a timetabled level-based regrouping (TaRL) hour in Grades 2–5 during the school day. We deliberately put camps first: in the RCT record, the intensive camp variant delivered up to 0.70 standard deviations while the mandated in-school variant (Haryana) delivered only 0.15 [25]; designing around the strong variant, with the in-school hour as reinforcement, follows the evidence rather than administrative convenience.
- **Mechanism:** Instruction at the child's actual level is the single most consistently replicated lever in the RCT literature (0.07–0.70 SD across six J-PAL/Pratham trials) [24][25]; Tamil Nadu showed the volunteer variant works at state scale post-COVID [34][35]. GEEAP rates this a "great buy" [26].
- **Target:** Rural government-school Std III children reading a Std II text: 23.4% (ASER 2024) → ~40% by ASER 2030 as our central estimate, with ≥50% as the stretch target under high-fidelity implementation; Std V division: 30.7% (ASER 2024) → ≥45% central, ≥55% stretch [1][6]. *Calibration:* effect sizes in standard deviations do not translate mechanically into ASER percentage points, so we anchor on observed system performance instead — UP roughly doubled its government-school Std III reading level between 2018 and 2024 from a low base [1][5]. Moving the national figure from 23.4% to 50% in six years requires UP-like improvement everywhere at once; the lower-bound in-school effect (0.15 SD) alone would deliver far less. The ≥50% figure therefore holds only under camp-level fidelity at scale, which is why we state ~40% as the central estimate.
- **Who implements:** DoSEL (Ministry of Education) sets the framework inside Samagra Shiksha; SCERTs adapt materials; districts run camps; CSOs (e.g., Pratham-type partners) train volunteers; panchayats recruit them.
- **Indicative cost:** Tamil Nadu's School Education Department reports ITK cost ₹660.35 crore across 2021-22 to 2024-25, against a cumulative reach of 95.97 lakh children over the programme's whole run [35] — both figures state-reported, not independently verified. That works out to roughly ₹690 per child *cumulatively*; the state has not published average annual enrolment, so a precise per-child-year figure cannot be computed (if the average child participated for 1.5–2 years, it implies roughly ₹350–450 per child-year). For national scale: UDISE+ 2024-25 records 10.44 crore children in Grades 1–5, with government schools holding roughly half of all enrolment (12.16 of 24.69 crore) [2][46], which puts government-school Grades 2–5 at roughly 4–4.5 crore children. Covering that band at ₹600–1,000 per child-year (allowing for higher-cost states, materials and camp delivery): **₹3,000–5,000 crore/year**, fundable within Samagra Shiksha's ₹42,100 crore (budget estimate 2026-27) [21] plus a top-up.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** TaRL effects collapse when implementation is nominal (the Haryana in-school variant gave only 0.15 SD vs 0.70 SD in well-run camps) [25]; volunteer churn; risk of narrowing teaching to tested basics. Mitigate with the measurement architecture in Intervention 4.

### Intervention 2 — Fill vacancies where they bind; manage presence, not just posts

- **Problem:** ~10 lakh vacant posts concentrated in a few states; secondary vacancies rose to 4.09 lakh in 2024-25 [11]; 1.04 lakh single-teacher schools [12]; 23.6% measured absence [13].
- **Intervention:** A centrally sponsored, 5-year matched grant for deficit states to recruit ~5 lakh regular teachers (priority: single-teacher schools and secondary subject teachers), conditional on (a) transparent, rule-based posting/transfer systems and (b) digital-plus-physical inspection regimes with published school-level presence data.
- **Mechanism:** The Andhra Pradesh contract-teacher RCT shows extra adults in classrooms raise learning (+0.16/0.15 SD maths/language over two years) largely via smaller effective class sizes, and that lower-paid contract teachers were no less effective than regular teachers [38] — but the Parliamentary Standing Committee documents how contractual hiring erodes reservation compliance and career structure [11], so we propose regular recruitment with the *governance* lessons (presence monitoring) attached: in the national absence study, villages with regular inspections had teacher absence 6.5 percentage points lower (roughly 17% against the 23.6% average) — an association in observational data, not an experimental result [13].
- **Target:** Vacancies: ~10 lakh (Standing Committee, Aug 2025) → <3 lakh; single-teacher schools: 1.04 lakh (UDISE+ 2024-25) → <25,000; published absence rate in an independent re-survey: 23.6% (c. 2010 baseline study) → <15% [11][12][13]. The absence target is deliberately conservative: the inspection correlation, taken at face value, would move absence from 23.6% to around 17%; anything below that depends on how monitoring, filled vacancies and posting reform stack — an assumption we cannot quantify from existing evidence.
- **Who implements:** State governments (recruitment is a state subject in practice); MoE/DoSEL designs the matched grant; District Institutes of Education and Training (DIETs) handle induction.
- **Indicative cost:** Bihar's 2023 BPSC recruitment (1.70 lakh posts advertised) pays a basic ₹25,000/month for primary teachers, ~₹28,000–32,000 for higher grades, before allowances [39] — i.e., roughly ₹4.5–6 lakh per teacher-year gross. But Bihar sits at the low end of state pay scales, which vary roughly two-fold, and vacancies are concentrated in specific states whose scales may run higher [11]. 5 lakh teachers ≈ **₹25,000–50,000 crore/year recurring** — the Bihar-scale floor to a higher-scale ceiling — phased in over five years, shared Centre:state. A weighted costing using the actual deficit states' pay scales is a first implementation task.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** This is the plan's dominant recurring cost and is irreversible (permanent salaries); class-size effects on learning are modest unless paired with pedagogy change [38]; and teacher transfer-and-posting reform has repeatedly stalled under political pressure in multiple states. Sequencing matters: tie tranche release to posting-reform milestones.

### Intervention 3 — Make the anganwadi a real pre-school

- **Problem:** Pre-primary enrolment is now 77.4% at age 3 (ASER 2024) [14], but the dominant provider — the anganwadi — has one ₹4,500/month worker juggling nutrition and education [16], and ECCE quality is unmeasured.
- **Intervention:** Phase in a second, ECCE-dedicated worker ("Balvatika educator") in the 2.9 lakh anganwadis already co-located with primary schools (MWCD/DoSEL co-location data, 2025) [45]; structured daily activity calendar and play materials; a public ECCE quality-assessment cycle under PARAKH.
- **Mechanism:** The directly relevant Indian experiment exists. A randomized evaluation across 640 Tamil Nadu anganwadis found that adding an extra worker dedicated to pre-school education roughly doubled instructional time and raised maths scores by 0.29 SD and language by 0.46 SD after 18 months, with lower rates of stunting and severe malnutrition; the authors' cost-benefit analysis finds benefits well above costs even under conservative assumptions [42]. GEEAP independently rates pre-primary among the highest-return "great buys" [26]; foundational deficits visible at Std III (Section 1) originate before Grade 1, and remediating later is costlier than preventing.
- **Target:** Primary target is quality, since that is what a second educator changes: first national ECCE quality baseline under PARAKH published by 2028, and at least 60% of sampled co-located Balvatikas meeting structured daily-activity benchmarks by 2030 (we propose this benchmark — no official one yet exists). Secondary indicator: age-3 pre-primary enrolment 77.4% (ASER 2024) → ≥90% (ASER 2030) [14] — noting that a second educator does not by itself recruit more 3-year-olds, so enrolment is a system-wide indicator, not this intervention's own yardstick.
- **Who implements:** Ministry of Women & Child Development (Saksham Anganwadi/POSHAN 2.0) jointly with DoSEL for the co-located Balvatikas; states hire educators; PARAKH/NCERT builds the assessment.
- **Indicative cost:** Phased across the 2.9 lakh co-located anganwadis [45]: educators at ~₹10,000/month ≈ ₹3,500 crore/year at full coverage, plus training/materials ≈ **₹4,000–6,000 crore/year** — against the current Saksham Anganwadi & POSHAN 2.0 envelope of ₹23,100 crore (budget estimate 2026-27) [15][16].
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Two-worker models can blur accountability; WCD–Education ministry coordination has historically been weak; honorarium-based hiring without social security (the current AWW model [16]) invites attrition and industrial action.

### Intervention 4 — Independent measurement plus Ceará-style incentives

- **Problem:** No consequence currently attaches to learning data; PARAKH 2024 exists but feeds no allocation decision [4]; the one robust governance correlate of teacher effort is being monitored [13].
- **Intervention:** (a) A legally mandated annual, independent, household-based FLN survey (ASER-style, government-funded but third-party-run) reported at district level; (b) convert 10–15% of Samagra Shiksha into a performance window paying states/districts for *independently measured gains* (not levels, which would punish poor states), modelled on Ceará's results-linked transfers [33]. Stated plainly: the window is funded by shrinking formula-based allocations, so in any given year some states will receive less than under the status quo.
- **Mechanism:** Sobral/Ceará are the best-documented case suggesting that frequent child-level assessment used formatively, plus money tied to results, can move a system from the bottom rank to the top within ~15 years [31][32][33] — but it is a single, uncontrolled case from one Brazilian state, and no Indian state has run results-linked education transfers (see Section 6.8). Household-based measurement is far harder to game than school-administered tests, though not immune — enumerator shirking, sample manipulation and coaching to the survey tool all become risks once money attaches — so independent fieldwork audits are part of the design.
- **Target:** Annual district FLN report cards published from 2027-28 onwards; ≥₹5,000 crore/year of Samagra Shiksha flowing through the performance window by 2029-30 (against ₹42,100 crore total, BE 2026-27) [21].
- **Who implements:** DoSEL legislates the survey mandate; PARAKH (NCERT) sets standards; an independent consortium (universities/CSOs) executes fieldwork; the Samagra Shiksha Project Approval Board administers the performance window.
- **Indicative cost:** Reliable public cost data for a national household learning survey is unavailable; for scale reference, PARAKH RS 2024 assessed 21.15 lakh students within NCERT's existing budget [8], and ASER surveys ~6.5 lakh children annually with volunteer enumerators [1]. We budget a conservative **₹300–500 crore/year** placeholder and flag it as the plan's least-certain cost line.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Measurement-linked money invites manipulation pressure — the design stands or falls on the survey body's independence. Gain-based payments can still disadvantage states hit by shocks (floods, elections, administrative churn), and because the window is carved out of existing formula allocations, some states lose money in bad years; its political durability likely depends on a hold-harmless floor, which we propose for years 1–2. Finally, teaching narrowly to the tested FLN metrics once money rides on them is a real risk (mitigate by rotating item banks and auditing).

### Intervention 5 — Hold the line at secondary

- **Problem:** Secondary NER is 47.5%, GER 68.5%, dropout 8.2% (UDISE+ 2024-25) [2][9]; vacancies are growing fastest at secondary [11]; girls face a device and ownership gap [17].
- **Intervention:** Targeted secondary completion package in the ~200 lowest-transition districts (identified from UDISE+ transition tables): subject-teacher recruitment (drawn from Intervention 2's pool), transport/bicycle and conditional scholarship support for girls, and remedial bridge courses at Grade 9 entry using Intervention 1's TaRL infrastructure.
- **Mechanism:** Dropout at secondary reflects compounding academic failure plus direct costs. Remediation addresses the first (ITK's recovery effects were largest for weaker students [34]). The transport component has direct Indian causal evidence: Bihar's Cycle programme — funds for a bicycle for every girl entering Grade 9 — raised girls' age-appropriate secondary enrolment by 32% (5.2 percentage points) and narrowed the gender enrolment gap by 40% in a rigorous quasi-experimental evaluation, at lower cost than comparable conditional cash transfers [43]. We target girls because the brief's own data show the gap runs against them (device ownership 26.9% versus boys' 36.2% [17]; girls' lower secondary participation in the lagging districts) — while acknowledging the trade-off that poor boys facing the same distance and cost barriers are excluded from this component. More broadly, India's fall in 15–16-year-old non-enrolment (13.1% → 7.9%, ASER 2018→2024 [10]) shows demand responds when access and support improve.
- **Target (programme districts):** In the ~200 programme districts: middle-to-secondary transition rate from each district's UDISE+ baseline to at least the current national average (86.6%), and district secondary dropout to <5%, by 2030-31 [2]. We deliberately do not set national targets for this intervention: a package in roughly 200 of India's ~780 districts cannot arithmetically move the national NER by more than a few percentage points even if it fully succeeds. Raising national secondary NER from 47.5% toward 60% is a whole-plan aspiration that depends on Interventions 1, 2 and 5 working together — and we flag it as an aspiration, not a costed projection [2][9].
- **Who implements:** States, via Samagra Shiksha's secondary component; MoE sets the district list; Panchayati Raj institutions verify beneficiary targeting.
- **Indicative cost:** Largely composed of cost lines above (teachers, remediation); incremental scholarships/transport in 200 districts ≈ **₹3,000–4,000 crore/year** (basis: extending existing Samagra Shiksha/National Means-cum-Merit-type per-student support; precise unit costs vary by state and we flag this as indicative).
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Cash/transport support without learning recovery produces enrolled-but-not-learning adolescents (the current Std VIII data [6]); conditionality enforcement is administratively heavy.

## 5. What it costs overall

Indicative incremental public cost at steady state (year 3 onwards), over and above current budgets:

| Intervention | ₹ crore/year (indicative) |
|---|---|
| 1. FLN + TaRL at scale | 3,000–5,000 |
| 2. Teacher recruitment + governance | 25,000–50,000 (Bihar-scale floor; see Intervention 2) |
| 3. ECCE/anganwadi upgrade | 4,000–6,000 |
| 4. Measurement + performance window | 300–500 (window reallocates existing funds) |
| 5. Secondary completion package | 3,000–4,000 |
| **Total incremental** | **≈ ₹35,000–65,000 crore/year** |

For scale: nominal GDP is ₹357.14 lakh crore in FY 2025-26 (First Advance Estimates, MoSPI, January 2026), with the FY 2026-27 budget assuming 10% nominal growth (≈ ₹393 lakh crore) [40][41]. The plan's steady-state increment is therefore roughly **0.09–0.17% of GDP per year** — against current combined public education spending of ~4–4.6% of GDP [18][19], a Department of School Education budget of ₹83,562 crore (budget estimate 2026-27) [21][22], and the NEP 2020 aspiration of 6% of GDP. Put differently: this plan consumes well under a fifth of the distance between current spending and the NEP target, and roughly three-quarters of it at the midpoint — the teacher salary line, between 70% and 76% across the range — would be incurred anyway if states comply with existing RTE norms on vacancies [11][12]. The Centre-state split matters: most recurring costs (salaries) fall on states, which already carry roughly three-quarters of public education expenditure [18].

## 6. What we don't know / where we could be wrong

**1. ASER and PARAKH disagree in level and cannot be reconciled exactly.** ASER is a rural household survey using a floor-level tool; PARAKH is a school-based, curriculum-referenced assessment [1][4]. Both show low learning, but neither gives a single "true" national number; our Std III baseline (23.4%) is government-schools-only and rural-only [1].

**2. TaRL's headline effects come from well-supervised implementations.** The 0.70 SD result is from intensive UP camps; the in-school Haryana variant delivered 0.15 SD [25]. At all-India scale, fidelity will be lower than in any RCT; our Intervention 1 central estimate (~40% Std III reading by 2030) assumes effect sizes nearer the lower bound, and the ≥50% stretch target assumes camp-level fidelity at national scale — we could still be too optimistic on both.

**3. The Tamil Nadu recovery attribution is partial.** The NBER study attributes ~24% of recovery to ITK; most recovery came from school reopening itself [34]. Scaling ITK-style programmes to non-crisis settings may yield smaller returns than the post-COVID episode suggests.

**4. The teacher-absence baseline is dated.** The 23.6% figure comes from fieldwork around 2010 (published 2017) [13]. Absence may have fallen with smartphone-based monitoring; no comparable nationally representative re-survey exists. Commissioning one is itself part of Intervention 4.

**5. Delhi's reforms lack causal identification.** Board pass-rate gains can reflect screening of weak students; the assessments showing reading gains were programme-administered [36][37]. We use Delhi as suggestive, not proof — and the same caution applies to UP's ASER surge, which has not been independently decomposed into NIPUN effects versus recovery effects [1][5].

**6. Vietnam's lesson may be smaller than it looks.** Up to 70% of Vietnam's outperformance is unexplained by observable policy variables [30]; importing its teacher standards may not import its results.

**7. Our cost lines are indicative, not budget-grade.** Teacher salary costs vary two-fold across states (Bihar's ₹25,000 basic is below many states' scales [39]) — which is why Intervention 2 carries a ₹25,000–50,000 crore range rather than a point estimate; the measurement line has no reliable public cost benchmark; ECCE educator costing assumes an honorarium model we simultaneously criticize. A detailed project report (DPR)-grade costing would be the first task of implementation.

**8. Performance-linked transfers rest on one case.** Intervention 4's incentive design is supported by a single Brazilian state's experience, documented in case studies, not controlled evaluations [31][32][33]. No Indian state has run results-linked education transfers; India's Centre-state fiscal politics differ sharply from Ceará's intra-state setting; and the design may prove politically unsurvivable — or be gamed in ways we have not anticipated — once ₹5,000 crore a year rides on survey results. This is the plan's riskiest and most novel component, which is why we pair it with a hold-harmless floor and independent fieldwork audits.

**9. The strongest counterargument to this plan:** that India's system fails not for lack of programmes but for lack of state capacity to run *any* programme with fidelity — in which case adding five new ones makes things worse, and the most defensible minimal version of this plan is the measurement-plus-incentives layer (Intervention 4) alone, letting states discover their own methods, as Ceará's municipalities did [33]. We weight the RCT evidence on TaRL and the ITK scale-up as showing Indian states *can* execute pedagogy reform [24][34] — but a reasonable person could read the record more pessimistically.

**10. Demand-side unknowns.** Falling overall enrolment (24.80 → 24.69 crore, 2023-24 → 2024-25 [2][3]) partly reflects demographic decline and data cleaning; we do not know how fertility-driven cohort shrinkage will change optimal school networks (consolidation versus single-teacher schools) over the plan period — a genuinely contested trade-off we have not costed.
