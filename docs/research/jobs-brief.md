# Jobs & Livelihoods — Evidence Brief

*Apni Sarkar | Flagship evidence brief | Last updated: June 2026*

India does not have a simple unemployment problem. The headline unemployment rate is low — 3.1% by the latest official survey [1]. The real problem is the *quality* of work: nine in ten Indian workers are informally employed [4], over half are self-employed (often in tiny, low-productivity family enterprises) [1], 43% of the workforce still works in agriculture which produces less than a fifth of national output [1][6], and real wages have barely moved in a decade [7]. Meanwhile, unemployment is concentrated brutally among the young and the educated: graduates face an unemployment rate roughly nine times that of workers who cannot read or write [4]. This brief lays out the evidence, the binding constraints, what has demonstrably worked elsewhere, and a costed five-year plan.

A note on data: the Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS) moved to a calendar-year cycle and a revised sampling frame from January 2025, so 2025 estimates are **not strictly comparable** with earlier annual rounds [1][2]. We state the survey vintage next to every number.

---

## 1. Where India stands

**Key indicators (with vintage and source):**

1. **Labour Force Participation Rate (LFPR): 59.3%** for persons aged 15+, usual status (ps+ss) — PLFS Annual Report 2025 (Jan–Dec 2025) [1]. It was 59.6% in calendar 2024 [2] and 60.1% in July 2023–June 2024 [3].

2. **Worker Population Ratio (WPR): 57.4%**, i.e. roughly 61.6 crore Indians employed (about 41.6 crore men, 20.0 crore women) — PLFS 2025 [1].

3. **Unemployment rate: 3.1%** (15+, usual status) in 2025 [1]; **4.9%** by the stricter current weekly status (CWS) measure in calendar 2024 [2]. The usual-status measure counts anyone who worked even part of the year as employed, which is why it looks low; CWS is closer to international practice.

4. **Youth unemployment (15–29): 9.9%** in 2025 — 13.6% urban, 8.3% rural (PLFS 2025) [1]. Youth made up **82.9% of India's unemployed** in 2022 (ILO India Employment Report 2024, using PLFS data) [4].

5. **Educated unemployment is the core of the crisis.** In 2022, the unemployment rate among youth with a graduate degree was **29.1%**, versus **3.4%** for those who cannot read and write — education raises unemployment risk because educated youth queue for scarce formal jobs (ILO, India Employment Report 2024) [4]. PLFS 2025 puts unemployment among the educated (secondary+, 15+) at 6.5%, down from 7.0% in 2024 [1] — this sub-figure does not appear in the PIB press release and should be verified against a direct page citation in the full PLFS 2025 PDF (MoSPI) before further citation.

6. **Female LFPR: 40.0%** (15+, usual status, PLFS 2025), against 79.1% for men [1]. It has risen substantially from 23.3% in 2017-18 to 41.7% in 2023-24 [3] — though the nature of that rise is contested (see Section 6.1). Rural female LFPR far exceeds urban [1]; the specific rural sub-figure from PLFS 2025 requires direct verification against the full MoSPI report PDF, not the PIB press release, before citation.

7. **Informality: ~90% of workers are informally employed**, and about 82% work in the informal sector (ILO, India Employment Report 2024) [4]. Only **23.6%** of workers had regular wage/salaried jobs in 2025 (up from 22.4% in 2024); **56.2%** were self-employed [1].

8. **Agriculture employs 43.0% of workers** (PLFS 2025) [1] while contributing only about **18% of Gross Value Added** (2023-24, Ministry of Agriculture statistical report) [6]. Manufacturing employs roughly 12% of workers [1] and has been stuck at roughly 14–17% of GVA for over a decade [5]; the National Manufacturing Mission targets 25% by 2035 [5].

9. **Real wages are nearly flat.** The ILO finds regular workers' real wages stagnated or declined between 2012 and 2022, and that the modest rise in monetary earnings has been eaten by inflation; only casual wages rose slightly in real terms [4][7]. As of 2018, **approximately 41% of casual workers and 15% of salaried workers were paid below the national minimum wage floor** (ILO India Wage Report, 2018) [8] — a more recent estimate is not publicly available, but given slow formalisation progress this situation is unlikely to have improved substantially.

10. **The job-creation requirement: ~78.5 lakh (7.85 million) non-farm jobs per year until 2030** just to absorb the growing workforce (Economic Survey 2023-24) [9].

11. **Skills deficit: only 4.2% of the workforce (15–59) has formal vocational/technical training** (PLFS 2025) [1]. **25% of youth (15–29) are NEET** — not in employment, education or training (PLFS 2025) [1].

12. **MSME credit gap: only ~19% of MSME credit demand is met formally**, leaving an unmet gap that NITI Aayog estimates at around ₹80 lakh crore (NITI Aayog, *Enhancing Competitiveness of MSMEs in India*, 2025) [10]; the earlier IFC estimate was ₹16.66 lakh crore (2018) [11]. These estimates use different definitions of eligible MSME credit demand and different data sources; the near five-fold gap between them reflects methodological divergence, not simply credit-gap growth. The NITI Aayog 2025 figure [10] is the more recent and should be treated as the operative benchmark, but it has not been independently reproduced.

13. **Gig and platform work: 77 lakh workers in 2020-21 (2.6% of the non-farm workforce), projected to reach 2.35 crore by 2029-30** (NITI Aayog, 2022) [12]. Over 31 crore unorganised workers were registered on e-Shram by late 2025 [13].

14. **Construction is now the second-largest employer** after agriculture, with roughly 7.1 crore workers — but only about 5.65 crore are registered under the Building and Other Construction Workers Act, leaving crores outside welfare coverage (The India Forum, citing official BOCW data) [14].

## 2. Root causes

**(a) Growth without labour absorption.** India's growth is led by services and capital-intensive industry, which generate output faster than jobs. Manufacturing's GVA share has stagnated around 14–17% [5], and within manufacturing the fastest-growing segments (electronics, petrochemicals, autos) employ relatively few workers per rupee of capital. India's GDP has grown at 6–8% annually through the 2010s and early 2020s. Over the same period, regular wage employment has grown only modestly — from 22.4% to 23.6% of workers between 2024 and 2025, though the PLFS frame change means this single-year shift should not be over-read [1][2]. The ILO's multi-year analysis finds that employment conditions improved least where output grew most [4], and real wages have stayed flat [4][7].

**(b) A "missing middle" of firms.** Most Indian enterprises are micro: self-employment is 56.2% of all work [1]. Firms stay small partly because formal credit is scarce — only ~19% of MSME credit demand is met formally [10] — and partly because regulatory and land/labour compliance costs rise sharply with size. Small firms cannot achieve the scale that made Bangladeshi garments or Vietnamese electronics globally competitive [15][16].

**(c) Education that doesn't connect to work.** Only 4.2% of the workforce has formal vocational training [1]. The flagship short-term skilling model has failed its own test: of 13.7 million trained under PMKVY in its first seven years, only ~18% were placed in jobs [17]; under PMKVY 3.0, fewer than 10% of certified candidates were recorded as placed per Parliamentary Standing Committee scrutiny [18] (the Careers360 report citing the committee gives a figure of 30,599 of ~4 lakh certified — treat as approximate, since the primary committee report was not independently verified), and the Committee has flagged that placement tracking was then dropped altogether under PMKVY 4.0, making verification impossible [18]. Apprenticeships — the proven bridge — cover ~0.1% of India's workforce versus ~4% in Germany [19].

**(d) Barriers specific to women.** Female LFPR (40.0%) is half the male rate (79.1%) [1]. Randomized evaluations across several LMICs find childcare access raises women's labour supply — but only where complementary barriers (safety, transport, norms, job availability) are also addressed [20]. India-specific experimental evidence on this channel is limited. India's public spending on the care economy is under 1% of GDP [21]. Where employers and states have solved housing and safety — e.g. Tamil Nadu's worker hostels — tens of thousands of women have entered factory work (Section 3).

**(e) Informality traps and thin safety nets for urban workers.** Rural India has had a statutory employment guarantee since 2005; urban India has none at national scale. Construction's 7+ crore workers are largely migrant and informal, with welfare boards reaching only a fraction [14]. Informal workers cannot smooth income shocks, so they cannot search for better jobs, invest in skills, or bargain for wages.

**(f) The educated-youth queue.** Because formal jobs are scarce and prized (government posts especially), educated youth remain unemployed for years rather than accept informal work — producing the inverted gradient where graduates have 29.1% unemployment and illiterate workers 3.4% [4]. This is a demand-side problem (too few good jobs), not primarily a supply-side one (too few "skilled" people), though both interact.

## 3. What has worked

### International benchmarks

**Bangladesh — garment-led female employment.** Bangladesh's ready-made garment (RMG) industry employs roughly 4 million workers, the majority women, and accounts for over 80% of export earnings and ~14% of GDP [15]. The sector pulled millions of rural women into formal wage work within a generation. *Caveats:* it was built on very low wages and weak labour protections (the Rana Plaza disaster forced reform); working conditions and harassment remain serious problems [15]. *Transferability:* India already has the textile base; what Bangladesh shows is that labour-intensive, export-oriented manufacturing can transform female employment at scale — if trade policy, logistics, and compliance costs allow firms to compete on the global price ladder.

**Vietnam — FDI-led manufacturing.** Foreign-invested firms account for ~98% of Vietnam's electronics exports, which hit US$126.5 billion in 2024 — a third of all exports [16]. Samsung accounted for approximately one-fifth of Vietnam's total exports in 2023 (OECD Viet Nam Survey 2025, using 2023 data) and employed ~112,000 workers; by 2024 this share had declined to approximately 13–15% as Vietnam's overall exports grew [16]. Foreign firms' share of Vietnam's formal workforce rose to ~35% by 2019, growing 5.6% a year since 1995 [16]. The playbook: aggressive trade-agreement coverage (CPTPP, EU-Vietnam FTA, RCEP), predictable land and customs administration, and single-window investor facilitation [16]. *Caveats:* OECD and RMIT analyses find weak spillovers to domestic firms — assembly without deep local value addition [16] — a critique also made of India's electronics PLI [22].

**Germany/Switzerland — dual apprenticeships.** Germany's dual vocational system places ~4% of the workforce in apprenticeships and underpins one of the OECD's lowest youth unemployment rates [19]. *Transferability caveat:* the German system rests on strong employer associations, codified occupational standards, and a mostly formal economy. With ~90% informality [4], India cannot copy it wholesale — but it can scale employer-led apprenticeships in its large formal firms and government bodies, where the model's preconditions exist.

**China — SEZs as job machines.** *Caveat first:* Chinese SEZs combined cheap land, suppressed labour rights, massive infrastructure and an undervalued currency — none of these are available or desirable for India. With that constraint in mind: Shenzhen grew from ~310,000 residents and under 30,000 workers in 1980 to 4.33 million people and a 3.09 million labour force by 2000, on the back of export manufacturing concentrated in a zone with liberalized rules [23]. The portable lesson is spatial concentration — bundling infrastructure, housing, and simplified compliance in a few places rather than spreading thin — a principle more directly illustrated by India's own SEZ and PCPIR (Petroleum, Chemicals and Petrochemicals Investment Region) experience.

### Indian state-level successes

**Tamil Nadu — women in factories.** Tamil Nadu hosts about 42% of India's registered women factory workers [24]. The state's electronics corridor demonstrates that treating safe accommodation as industrial infrastructure rather than welfare unlocks women's factory employment at scale: Foxconn's Sriperumbudur iPhone plant employed 41,281 workers, of whom 33,360 were women [25], and a ₹706.50-crore SIPCOT industrial housing facility for 18,720 women workers was built at the cluster [26]. The same model has produced documented rights violations — exclusion of married women from hiring and restrictive hostel conditions [25] — that must be treated as design constraints, not footnotes, in any replication attempt.

**MGNREGA — the employment guarantee evidence base.** India's own rural employment guarantee is among the world's best-studied anti-poverty programs. Imbert & Papp (2015) estimated that NREGA raised low-skill rural wages by approximately 5% on average, with effects concentrated in the seven states that implemented the programme most actively — these estimates are based on the NREGA rollout period (2005–2008), and wage effects at current scale may differ [27]. Muralidharan, Niehaus & Sukhtankar (2017) showed that fixing payments (biometric smartcards in Andhra Pradesh) produced large gains in earnings and assets — implementation quality, not design, was the constraint [27]. *Status note:* In the Union Budget 2026-27, MGNREGA allocations were reduced to ₹30,000 crore alongside the new VB-G RAM G rural employment mission (₹95,692 crore) [28]. Whether the VB-G RAM G preserves the demand-driven guarantee structure of MGNREGA is not yet clear from published rules; until that is established, comparison of headline allocations is not a reliable guide to effective coverage.

**Rajasthan — the urban employment guarantee pilot.** The Indira Gandhi Shahari Rozgar Guarantee Yojana (launched September 2022 at 100 days per household; expanded to 125 days from April 2023) guarantees 125 days of urban wage work per household. Field evaluations (The India Forum field study; *Development in Practice*, 2025) find the scheme primarily attracts women workers because of its flexibility, that demand for work exceeds supply, and that the main failures are payment delays and low wage rates — implementation problems, not absence of demand [29]. This is the closest India has to a tested template for an urban safety net.

## 4. The 5-year plan

> Throughout: "evidence shows" claims are cited; the proposals themselves are our synthesis and are flagged as such. Costs are indicative, anchored to cited cost bases. Nominal GDP for FY 2025-26 is estimated at ₹357.14 lakh crore (NSO First Advance Estimates) [30]; we use this as the denominator.

### Intervention 1 — Reorient manufacturing incentives from capital to jobs

- **Problem:** Incentives reward investment and output, not employment. PLI disbursements flowed disproportionately to capital-intensive sectors; the Economic Survey 2025-26 credits PLI with ₹2.0 lakh crore of investment but only 12.6 lakh jobs [31] — roughly ₹1.6 crore of investment per job. Academic economists have questioned whether PLI generates genuine value addition or primarily subsidises assembly [22]; defenders argue it builds anchor investments and supply-chain depth over longer horizons. The evidence base for either claim at this scale is still accumulating.
- **Intervention (we propose):** Make the new Employment Linked Incentive (ELI) scheme — ₹99,446 crore, targeting 3.5 crore jobs incentivised over Aug 2025–Jul 2027 via EPFO-verified payroll [32] — the template for *all* manufacturing support: pay for verified, sustained payroll additions, with higher rates for women workers and labour-intensive sectors (apparel, footwear, food processing, toys, furniture, electronics assembly).
- **Mechanism:** Wage subsidies lower the marginal cost of hiring exactly where India has comparative advantage (abundant semi-skilled labour); EPFO verification makes the subsidy formalising by construction.
- **Target:** Manufacturing employment share from ~12% (PLFS 2025 [1]) to 14% by 2031; at least 40% of incentivised new jobs going to women (baseline: women are ~20.0 crore of 61.6 crore workers overall [1]).
- **Who implements:** Ministry of Labour & Employment + EPFO (payments); Ministry of Commerce & DPIIT (sector schemes); states for land/single-window.
- **Indicative cost:** ELI is already budgeted at ₹99,446 crore over ~2 years [32] (~0.14% of GDP per year [30]). We propose sustaining this envelope for 5 years: ~₹2.5 lakh crore total.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Deadweight (paying for jobs that would exist anyway); churn gaming (fire-and-rehire); evidence on the predecessor PMRPY's additionality is thin. Mitigation: independent evaluation with EPFO microdata (the Employees' Provident Fund Organisation's payroll records, which are the verification backbone of ELI) published annually.

### Intervention 2 — A phased urban employment guarantee

- **Problem:** Urban casual and gig workers have no income floor; urban youth unemployment is 13.6% [1]. Rajasthan's pilot shows demand for urban public work exists, especially among women [29].
- **Intervention (we propose):** A national urban works program in towns under 1 million population, starting at 50 days/household and scaling on evidence — adapting the Azim Premji University Centre for Sustainable Employment design (100 days at a statutory wage, with works covering urban commons, care, and civic services) [33].
- **Mechanism:** The MGNREGA literature shows employment guarantees raise market wages for the poorest and self-target effectively [27]; Rajasthan shows urban demand and women's uptake [29].
- **Target:** 1 crore urban households getting at least 30 days of work per year by Year 5 (baseline: no national program; Rajasthan-type state schemes only [29]).
- **Who implements:** Ministry of Housing & Urban Affairs; urban local bodies execute; states co-fund (the APU design proposes Centre:state 80:20 on labour costs [33]).
- **Indicative cost:** The full APU national design was costed at 1.7–2.7% of GDP [33]. A 50-day, small-town, phased version is far smaller: we propose ramping from ₹20,000 crore/year to ₹60,000 crore/year by Year 5 (~0.06→0.17% of FY26 GDP [30]). This is our scaling of the APU cost basis, not an independently verified estimate. Note: the APU design assumed a ₹500/day statutory wage in 2019 prices; applying rough CPI inflation since then suggests a 2026 equivalent of ₹600–700/day, which would scale costs proportionally upward. Our phased estimate should be treated as a lower bound.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Urban works are harder to supervise than rural earthworks; wage-setting can distort low-end labour markets; payment delays destroyed value in Rajasthan [29]. Mitigation: start in small towns, third-party concurrent evaluation, payments via the MGNREGA smartcard lessons [27]. Additionally: if urban informal wages in target cities already exceed the statutory guarantee rate, take-up will be low and cost-per-job will rise sharply. Conversely, in cities with deep labour surplus, a guarantee wage could set a de facto floor above private-sector clearing rates, raising employer costs and potentially suppressing informal hiring. Neither effect is modelled here; preliminary wage-mapping by city class should precede national design.

### Intervention 3 — Apprenticeships as the default school-to-work bridge

- **Problem:** Only 4.2% of the workforce has formal vocational training [1]; apprentices are ~0.1% of the workforce versus ~4% in Germany [19]. Short-term classroom skilling has demonstrably failed to place people: ~18% placement under PMKVY's first seven years [17][18].
- **Intervention (we propose):** Shift the marginal skilling rupee from short-term training to employer-led apprenticeships: raise NAPS stipend cost-sharing, simplify compliance for MSMEs, mandate apprenticeship quotas in PSUs and large government contractors, and publish placement/retention outcomes per establishment. NAPS engaged ~42 lakh apprentices cumulatively over FY2018-19 to mid-2025 [19] — the pipeline exists but is small relative to ~7.85 million annual workforce entrants [9].
- **Mechanism:** Apprenticeships solve the information problem (employers observe workers before hiring) and the relevance problem (training happens on real equipment). Germany's low youth unemployment is built on this [19], with the transferability caveat that India must concentrate on its formal-sector islands first.
- **Target:** From ~6–7 lakh apprentices engaged per year (implied by dividing the cumulative NAPS total of ~42 lakh over roughly seven years — not a stated current-year figure; if recent annual engagement has been rising, the baseline may be higher and the target proportionally less ambitious) to 30 lakh per year by Year 5; placement-or-retention rate published quarterly (baseline: placement data currently not even uploaded, per the Standing Committee [18]). Note: the Ministry should publish annual enrolment by year to allow a clean baseline.
- **Who implements:** Ministry of Skill Development & Entrepreneurship (NAPS); industry bodies; state apprenticeship cells.
- **Indicative cost:** At NAPS-2 stipend support of ₹1,500/month per apprentice for ~11 months [19], 30 lakh apprentices/year ≈ ₹5,000 crore/year of central stipend support — ~₹25,000 crore over 5 years (our arithmetic on the cited stipend rate).
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Apprentice substitution for regular workers (firms cycling cheap apprentice labour); quality collapse if numbers are chased. Mitigation: cap apprentice-to-worker ratios, audit completion and retention, not enrolment.

### Intervention 4 — A national mission for women's work: childcare, housing, transport

- **Problem:** Female LFPR is 40.0% vs 79.1% for men [1], and the recent rise is concentrated in unpaid family work and own-account work, not wage jobs [34]. Public care-economy spending is under 1% of GDP [21].
- **Intervention (we propose):** (i) Universal public childcare in urban wards and industrial clusters (expanding Palna/Anganwadi-cum-crèche); (ii) state-built women's worker hostels at industrial nodes on the Tamil Nadu SIPCOT model [26]; (iii) safe-transport corridors to factory and service clusters.
- **Mechanism:** Nine randomized evaluations across eight LMICs find childcare access raises women's labour supply where complementary barriers are addressed [20]; Tamil Nadu demonstrates the housing/safety channel at industrial scale (33,360 women in one plant [25], 42% of India's women factory workers in one state [24]). The ILO estimates that direct public investment of 2% of GDP in care services could generate ~11 million jobs in India, ~70% going to women [21] — care is both an enabler and an employer.
- **Target:** We target measurable progress in female regular-wage share — a harder indicator to game than LFPR alone and more closely tracking the policy's intent (baseline: regular wage = 23.6% of all workers [1]; track the female sub-share specifically). A specific LFPR point-estimate target (e.g. 46% by 2031) is not derived here from any underlying model; the primary co-target is the female regular-wage share, which is what the interventions are designed to move.
- **Who implements:** Ministry of Women & Child Development (childcare); state industrial development corporations (hostels, on the ₹706.50 crore/18,720-bed SIPCOT cost basis ≈ ₹3.8 lakh/bed [26]); urban local bodies + state transport undertakings.
- **Indicative cost:** Phasing public care spending up by 0.25% of GDP per year — roughly ₹90,000 crore/year by Year 5 against the FY26 GDP base [30] — toward the ILO's 2%-of-GDP scenario [21]; plus, using the SIPCOT unit cost as a ceiling (~₹3.8 lakh/bed [26]), 50 projects at 10,000 beds each would cost roughly ₹19,000 crore — treat as order-of-magnitude only.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Childcare raises participation only where jobs and safety exist [20] — sequencing matters; hostels can become instruments of control over women workers if unregulated [25]. Mitigation: tie hostel funding to labour-standard audits; co-locate childcare with employment clusters.

### Intervention 5 — Close the MSME credit and formalization gap

- **Problem:** Only ~19% of MSME credit demand is met formally; the gap is estimated near ₹80 lakh crore [10]. Credit-starved micro-firms cannot grow into employers; 56.2% of workers remain self-employed [1].
- **Intervention (we propose):** Triple the corpus and coverage of credit guarantees (CGTMSE), make Udyam + e-Shram + GST data interoperable for cash-flow-based lending, and tie guarantee pricing to verified employment growth in borrower firms.
- **Mechanism:** The constraint is collateral and information, not bankability: between 2020 and 2024 the share of micro/small enterprises accessing scheduled-bank credit rose from 14% to 20% as digital underwriting spread [10] — guarantee capacity and data plumbing scale that trend.
- **Target:** Formal credit meeting 35% of MSME credit demand by 2031 (baseline 19%, NITI Aayog 2025 [10]).
- **Who implements:** Ministry of MSME, SIDBI, RBI (regulatory), banks/NBFCs.
- **Indicative cost:** Credit guarantees are contingent liabilities, not outlays; provisioning of ~₹10,000–15,000 crore/year against expanded guarantee cover (our estimate, treat as illustrative). Calibration anchor: CGTMSE's published annual reports show guarantee volume, claims paid, and corpus size that imply a rough provisioning rate of ~3–5% of guarantee cover annually — our range is broadly consistent with that ratio applied to a larger corpus, though CGTMSE's exact provisioning methodology is not publicly standardised. Reliable public data on required provisioning rates is limited and this range should not be taken as an audited figure.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Guarantee schemes can breed moral hazard and NPAs; employment-linked pricing adds verification burden. Mitigation: risk-based pricing, public NPA reporting by cohort.

### Intervention 6 — Make the labour data and welfare floor portable

- **Problem:** 7.1 crore construction workers, only ~5.65 crore registered for welfare [14]; gig workers (77 lakh in 2020-21, heading to 2.35 crore by 2029-30 [12]) sit outside most social insurance; 31 crore e-Shram registrations exist but link weakly to benefits [13].
- **Intervention (we propose):** A portable benefits stack on e-Shram (the national registry for unorganised workers): accident and health cover, pension contributions co-paid by platforms/contractors, BOCW cess (a 1% levy on construction project costs, collected by state welfare boards but historically under-spent) actually disbursed to registered workers, and PLFS-linked district labour dashboards published monthly.
- **Mechanism:** Portability removes the penalty informal workers pay for mobility; the Code on Social Security (2020) already provides the legal hooks for gig-worker contributions [12]. Registration without benefits has no behavioural effect; benefits make registration self-sustaining.
- **Target:** 90% of BOCW-eligible workers registered and at least one benefit disbursed (baseline 5.65/7.1 crore registered, fraction receiving benefits unknown [14]); all major platforms remitting social-security contributions by Year 3.
- **Who implements:** Ministry of Labour & Employment; state BOCW boards; platform aggregators.
- **Indicative cost:** Largely financed by the existing BOCW cess (1% of construction cost — collected but under-spent [14]) and platform contributions; central tech and administration ~₹2,000 crore over 5 years (our estimate, flagged as such).
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Cess-funded boards have a two-decade record of under-spending [14]; platform contribution mandates may be passed on to workers as lower pay. Mitigation: statutory spending floors, transparent board accounts.

## 5. What it costs overall

Annual incremental public cost at full ramp (Year 5), against FY 2025-26 nominal GDP of ₹357.14 lakh crore [30]:

| Intervention | Year-5 annual cost | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Employment-linked incentives (sustained ELI) | ~₹50,000 crore | ELI outlay ₹99,446 crore/~2 years [32] |
| Urban employment guarantee (phased) | ~₹60,000 crore | Scaled from APU 1.7–2.7%-of-GDP full design [33] |
| Apprenticeships (NAPS expansion) | ~₹5,000 crore | NAPS-2 stipend rates [19] |
| Women's work mission (care + hostels + transport) | ~₹90,000 crore | ILO 2%-of-GDP care scenario, phased [21]; SIPCOT hostel unit cost [26] |
| MSME guarantee provisioning | ~₹12,500 crore | Illustrative; see Section 4 |
| Portable benefits / data | ~₹500 crore | Our estimate |
| **Total (Year 5)** | **~₹2.2 lakh crore/year** | **~0.6% of FY26 GDP [30]** |

For comparison: the Budget 2026-27 already allocates ₹95,692 crore to VB-G RAM G plus ₹30,000 crore to MGNREGA — about ₹1.26 lakh crore (~0.35% of FY26 GDP) on rural employment guarantee alone [28][30]; ELI's ₹99,446 crore is committed through July 2027 [32]. So roughly half of the proposed Year-5 envelope is re-orientation and continuation of existing commitments rather than new money; the genuinely new spending (urban guarantee + care economy) is on the order of 0.3–0.4% of GDP per year, phased over five years. Early years cost far less (~₹60,000–80,000 crore incremental).

## 6. What we don't know / where we could be wrong

1. **The female LFPR rise may be partly a measurement artefact or distress, not opportunity.** The increase since 2017-18 is concentrated in unpaid family helpers (9.1%→19.6%) and own-account work (4.5%→14.6%), not wage jobs [34]. CEDA and EPW analyses argue part reflects better counting of unpaid work and part may reflect household income distress [34]. The female regular-wage share is therefore the more meaningful target — and we have specified it as the primary co-target in Intervention 4.

2. **PLFS 2025 broke the series.** The new sampling frame means 2025 levels are not strictly comparable with 2023-24 [1][2]. Some of the apparent changes (e.g. rural female WPR movements) may be frame effects. Every trend statement spanning 2024→2025 in this brief should be read with that caveat.

3. **The low headline unemployment rate is a definitional choice.** Usual-status UR (3.1% [1]) coexists with CWS UR near 4.9% [2] and very high youth/graduate unemployment [4]. The two measures use different reference periods (past year vs past week) and produce different results by construction — neither is wrong; they measure different things. We present both without adjudicating which better captures labour market distress, as that determination would require access to unit-level PLFS microdata and an agreed welfare framework.

4. **Wage-subsidy additionality is unproven at ELI's scale.** There is no published independent evaluation showing how many EPFO-incentivised jobs are genuinely additional. Our Intervention 1 could partly be a transfer to firms that would have hired anyway. The 12.6 lakh PLI jobs figure [31] is a government claim, not an audited count.

5. **Urban employment guarantee evidence is thin.** Rajasthan's scheme has field studies, not randomized or quasi-experimental impact estimates [29]. Urban works may not replicate MGNREGA's wage effects [27] because urban labour markets are deeper. Our phased design is a hedge against exactly this uncertainty, but the cost-benefit case at national scale is unproven.

6. **Transferability of all four international benchmarks is partial.** Bangladesh's RMG model rode a low-wage, weak-protection phase India should not emulate [15]; Vietnam's electronics boom shows weak domestic spillovers [16]; Germany's apprenticeship preconditions (formality, employer associations) are mostly absent in India [4][19]; Shenzhen's land-and-labour model is institutionally unavailable [23]. We use these as directional evidence, not blueprints.

7. **The MGNREGA→VB-G RAM G transition is a live unknown.** The 2026-27 budget shift [28] could strengthen or weaken the rural income floor depending on rules not yet public (state coverage, 40% state cost-share, days guaranteed). Our plan assumes a functioning rural guarantee persists; if it doesn't, rural distress migration would change the urban-guarantee arithmetic.

8. **Strongest counterargument to our plan:** that India's job problem is fundamentally a *productivity and firm-size* problem, and 0.6% of GDP spent on guarantees, subsidies and care will treat symptoms while delaying factor-market reforms (land, labour codes implementation, tariffs) that would let large labour-intensive firms emerge. We partly accept this: Interventions 1, 3 and 5 are designed to be complements to, not substitutes for, those reforms — but if fiscal money crowds out reform attention, the critics would be right.

9. **Interventions 1 and 2 may partially cancel each other.** ELI (Intervention 1) lowers the marginal cost of formal hiring at a given wage; an urban employment guarantee (Intervention 2) raises the informal reservation wage. In low-wage urban labour markets both effects could be simultaneously active, compressing ELI's wage-subsidy advantage. The net effect depends on how the ELI wage threshold and the guarantee wage are calibrated against local wage distributions — a sensitivity analysis should be done before the final design of either scheme is locked.

10. **Several cost figures are our arithmetic on cited unit costs** (hostel scaling, apprentice stipends, urban-guarantee phasing, MSME provisioning) and are flagged as such in Section 4. Reliable recent data on BOCW cess utilisation rates and PMKVY 4.0 placement is unavailable [14][18] — itself a finding.
