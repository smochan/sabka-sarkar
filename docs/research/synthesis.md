## Section 3: Methodology Note

### How these briefs were produced

Each brief — health, education, jobs, and sortition — is built on a four-tier source hierarchy. The first tier is official Indian government statistics: survey reports published by the Office of the Registrar General of India (the Sample Registration System and its maternal mortality bulletins), the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare's National Health Accounts, the Ministry of Education's UDISE+ school data, and the Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation's Periodic Labour Force Survey. These are the foundation because they are nationally representative, methodologically documented, and publicly accessible; they are cited with their survey years, not just publication years, because the fieldwork vintage is what determines data age.

The second tier is multilateral data: OECD, World Bank, and ILO publications, including global learning databases and the ILO's India Employment Report. These are used where no equivalent Indian series exists (comparative health spending across countries, PISA scores, OECD's deliberative democracy database) or where they consolidate and validate the official series with independent methodology.

The third tier is peer-reviewed research and independent evaluations: randomised controlled trials, quasi-experimental studies, and formally evaluated programme assessments published in academic journals or as working papers of standing research institutions. This tier is where effect sizes and causal claims are grounded. All such claims carry the study design in the text — "RCT", "panel study", "quasi-experimental" — because observational associations and experimental estimates are not interchangeable.

Data journalism and advocacy-organisation reports are used only as pointers to the primary statistical release when that release is otherwise inaccessible or has no direct URL. They are never used as the underlying evidence for a factual claim.

### How targets were set

Every target in the five-year plans names its baseline, the measurement instrument used to establish that baseline, and the year the baseline was measured. Targets that depend on a single study or a programme-internal evaluation are flagged as such and accompanied by a lower-bound estimate. Where effect sizes in standard deviations are converted to projected ASER percentage points or PLFS share-points, the brief explicitly notes that the conversion is not mechanical and states the assumptions behind it.

### How claims were verified

Each brief underwent an automated fact-check pass — every statistic was traced to a citable primary source — followed by an adversarial editorial review that challenged causal claims, checked for internal consistency across sections, and compared stated figures with the sources themselves. A revision pass resolved flagged discrepancies and added caveats where evidence was contested or vintage was stale.

## Section 4: Cross-Cutting Themes

### State capacity is the binding constraint, not just the budget

All three sector briefs return to the same bottleneck: the ability of state governments and their district machinery to absorb and deploy money effectively. In health, the National Health Mission has chronically left funds unspent in the states that most need them — CAG documentation shows state treasuries taking fifty to two hundred and seventy-one days to transfer released funds to health societies, and NIPFP analysis shows low utilisation concentrated in the eight Empowered Action Group states with the worst health outcomes. In education, the Parliamentary Standing Committee flags roughly ten lakh vacant teaching posts despite a workforce of one crore that is, in aggregate, above the required pupil-teacher ratio — the problem is distribution and posting, not headcount. In jobs, the flagship short-term skilling programme placed fewer than one in five trained candidates in its first seven years, and placement tracking was then dropped entirely under its fourth iteration. The pattern is consistent: India can design programmes, fund them, and measure inputs. It struggles to translate budgeted posts into filled posts, filled posts into services, and services into outcomes. Any plan that responds to this by simply adding more programme layers is likely to produce more lapsed budgets, not better outcomes.

### Centre-state federalism as the lever, not the obstacle

Each brief's five-year plan is structured around the Centre-state implementation split because the Constitution assigns health, education, and labour to the Concurrent List, with states carrying the bulk of service delivery. The health brief proposes conditional grants that release NHM funds only when states meet treasury-transfer and vacancy-filling milestones. The education brief proposes a matched grant for deficit-state teacher recruitment tied to posting-reform milestones. The jobs brief notes that most recurring employment costs — teacher salaries, MGNREGA works, urban guarantee wages — fall on states, which are already carrying roughly three-quarters of public education expenditure. The sortition brief finds that gram-sabha pilots require only a state government order to launch; nothing national is needed. The implication is uncomfortable: for eight to ten large states in the Hindi heartland and the northeast, where outcomes are worst and capacity thinnest, each plan's feasibility must be stress-tested against those specific states, not the national average. A programme that works in Tamil Nadu and Kerala and fails in Uttar Pradesh and Bihar succeeds where it is least needed.

### The fiscal envelope and its arithmetic

The three sector briefs each include a total cost estimate. Health proposes raising government health expenditure from approximately 1.9% of GDP (budgeted, 2024-25) to 2.5% of GDP by FY2031 — an increment of roughly 0.6 percentage points of GDP per year at maturity, or approximately ₹2.0 lakh crore per year in FY25 terms against India's nominal GDP of ₹330.68 lakh crore. Education's incremental cost at steady state is estimated at ₹35,000–65,000 crore per year, or roughly 0.09–0.17% of FY26 nominal GDP (₹357.14 lakh crore). The jobs plan's Year-5 annual cost is estimated at approximately ₹2.2 lakh crore per year, or about 0.6% of FY26 GDP — though roughly half of that reorients existing commitments (ELI, MGNREGA successor) rather than creating new spending. The sortition programme is an order of magnitude smaller: ₹130–250 crore over five years, well under 0.001% of annual GDP. Adding the three sector increments together: the plan in its entirety costs roughly 1.3–1.4% of GDP per year at steady state above current spending. India currently spends approximately 2.1% of GDP on health and education combined at the central level, and significantly more at the state level. The combined increment is large but not extraordinary by the standards of comparable middle-income countries that have achieved universal health coverage or upper-middle-income literacy rates — Thailand and Vietnam both reached those outcomes at comparable or lower GDP per capita, though both benefited from smaller populations and, in Thailand's case, from decades of district-hospital construction before the coverage expansion began.

### The political economy of absorption failure

Across all three sector briefs, a structural explanation emerges for why state capacity gaps persist despite repeated programme cycles: complementary inputs are missing simultaneously. In health, NHM funds cannot be absorbed where trained staff are not in post, facilities are not built, and procurement systems do not exist — each requires the others before any one of them produces health outcomes. In education, teacher vacancies cannot be filled where posting rules are opaque, rural postings carry no career incentive, and transfer markets are dominated by political intermediaries. In jobs, apprenticeships cannot scale where employers are too small to certify, where the formal sector is too thin to absorb certified workers, and where placement data is not even collected. The common thread is that governance reform and resource increase must move together; sequencing either first is the dominant academic and political debate, and neither brief claims to resolve it. What each brief does say is that conditional financing — money contingent on measurable institutional improvements — is the design tool most likely to move both simultaneously.

### The measurement deficit is itself a finding

All three sector briefs flag data gaps that are not incidental but structural. NFHS-6 has not been released as of June 2026; the most recent nationally representative nutrition data is from 2019-21 fieldwork. The teacher-absence study that every education reform cites has fieldwork from around 2010. The PLFS changed its sampling frame in January 2025, breaking comparability with all prior annual rounds. BOCW cess utilisation rates are not publicly reported in a standard annual series. The PARAKH Rashtriya Sarvekshan exists but its results are not yet tied to any resource allocation decision. In each case the measurement gap is not merely an inconvenience for research: it is evidence that the accountability infrastructure — independent measurement tied to consequences — is itself the missing public good that would do the most to drive the others.

## Section 5: Honest Limitations

These briefs are evidence summaries, not policy guarantees, and several limitations are structural rather than fixable with better research.

**Data vintage.** The most important Indian health and nutrition survey, NFHS-5, has fieldwork ending in 2021. As of June 2026, no NFHS-6 national report exists. Anaemia, stunting, and service-coverage figures in the health brief are therefore five-plus years old. For education, ASER 2024 is genuinely current, but the teacher-absence study underpinning the governance analysis has fieldwork from around 2010. The PLFS changed its sampling frame in January 2025, so year-on-year comparisons spanning that break should be treated with caution.

**Observational versus experimental evidence.** Most of the international comparisons — Thailand's OOP reduction, Brazil's IMR decline, Kerala's participatory planning results, Tamil Nadu's health outcomes — are correlational or quasi-experimental, not randomised. Confounders, selection effects, and the specifics of those states' institutional histories limit how directly their success can be attributed to the policies described, and how safely those policies can be transplanted.

**India's heterogeneity problem.** A national average is, for most of India's policy problems, the least useful number. The seven-fold spread in infant mortality between Kerala (5 per 1,000) and Chhattisgarh/UP/Madhya Pradesh (37 per 1,000) is the actual policy problem. Plans calibrated to the national average will under-invest where the need is greatest and over-invest where capacity to use additional resources is already strained. Every target in every brief should be disaggregated to the state and district level before implementation begins.

**"Evidence brief" is not "policy guarantee."** An evidence brief can establish that a particular intervention produced a measured result in a particular context. It cannot guarantee that the same intervention, run by different implementing agencies in different political economies, will produce the same result. The briefs are honest about this: effect sizes from well-supervised RCTs collapse in under-supervised replications; Kerala's People's Plan momentum declined after its initial campaign phase; France's citizens' assembly produced a climate law that largely ignored its own recommendations. The honest purpose of an evidence brief is to narrow the range of plausible outcomes and identify the conditions under which programmes fail — not to eliminate uncertainty.
