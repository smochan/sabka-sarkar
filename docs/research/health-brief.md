# Health — Evidence Brief

*Apni Sarkar | Flagship evidence brief | Last updated: June 2026*

Infant mortality has reached its lowest recorded level — 25 per 1,000 — but a child in Chhattisgarh is seven times more likely to die before age one than a child in Kerala. Maternal deaths have fallen by a third since 2014-16, yet Assam's maternal mortality ratio remains among the worst in the region. Government health spending has risen as a share of GDP, but the most recent data show that post-pandemic, public spending has retreated and households are again paying more out of pocket. These are not two separate stories; they are the same story at different stages. This brief lays out where India stands, why, what has demonstrably worked, and a costed five-year plan. Every number carries its survey year. Where evidence is contested, we say so.

---

## 1. Where India stands

**Money.**

1. **Total health expenditure (THE)** was ₹9,04,461 crore, or **3.83% of GDP**, and ₹6,602 per capita in 2021-22 [1][2]. The most recent National Health Accounts (NHA) release is **NHA 2022-23**, published by the Union Health Ministry in May 2026 [1a].
2. **Government health expenditure (GHE)** was **1.43% of GDP in 2022-23** (NHA 2022-23) [1a], down from 1.84% in 2021-22 [1][2] — a post-pandemic reversal as COVID-driven public spending normalised. The government's share of total health spending peaked at **48.0% in FY22** (Economic Survey 2024-25) [3] and fell back to **43.7% in FY22-23** (NHA 2022-23) [1a]. This is well short of the National Health Policy 2017 target of 2.5% of GDP by 2025; combined centre-plus-state budgeted health spending was about 1.9% of GDP in 2024-25 (PRS analysis) [4]. *Note: the FY22 peak of 48% government share reflected COVID-era exceptional public spending — it was not a structural achievement.*
3. **Out-of-pocket expenditure (OOPE)** was **43.4% of THE in 2022-23** (NHA 2022-23) [1a], up from 39.4% in the COVID-distorted year 2021-22 [1][2]. The longer-term trend remains an improvement from 64.2% in 2013-14, but NHA 2022-23 confirms the post-pandemic reversal that peer-reviewed estimates had flagged: the 47-49% OOPE range [30] reported for adjacent non-COVID years was closer to the structural reality than the 39.4% pandemic-year figure. Indian households still pay a larger share of health costs directly than households in most middle-income countries.
4. **Union Budget 2026-27** allocates **₹1,06,530 crore to the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare** (about 10% above the FY26 revised estimate), including ₹39,390 crore for the National Health Mission, ₹9,500 crore for PM-JAY and ₹4,770 crore for PM-ABHIM [5].

**Mortality.**

5. **Infant mortality rate (IMR): 25 per 1,000 live births in 2023** (SRS Statistical Report 2023, released September 2025), down from 32 in 2018. Kerala is at 5; Chhattisgarh, Madhya Pradesh and Uttar Pradesh are at 37 — a seven-fold spread [6].
6. **Maternal mortality ratio (MMR): 88 per 100,000 live births in 2020-22** (SRS Special Bulletin on Maternal Mortality, released 2025), down from 130 in 2014-16 [7]; the previous bulletin recorded 93 for 2019-21 [8]. Assam's MMR remains several times Kerala's [7] — the interstate gap is the central fact of Indian maternal health.
7. **Under-five mortality: 31 per 1,000 (2021)** and **neonatal mortality: 19 per 1,000 (2021)**, per SRS as cited by the Ministry of Health [8]. Neonatal deaths — concentrated in the first month of life and dependent on facility quality, not just outreach — now dominate child mortality.

**Disease burden and nutrition.**

8. **Non-communicable diseases (NCDs) cause roughly 62% of deaths in India** (GBD 2016 estimate: 61.8%), up from about 38% in 1990 (37.9%) (ICMR–PHFI–IHME "India: Health of the Nation's States", GBD 2016 estimates, cited by the Health Ministry) [12]. Cardiovascular disease, diabetes, chronic respiratory disease and cancers are now the main killers — yet the public system was built for infections and childbirth.
9. **NFHS-5 (2019-21):** 35.5% of children under five are stunted; **67% of children aged 6-59 months and 57% of women 15-49 are anaemic** — anaemia *worsened* relative to NFHS-4 (2015-16: 59% and 53%) [9]. *(Note: the capillary measurement method used may overstate absolute anaemia levels — see Section 6, point 4 — but the worsening trend between surveys is harder to dismiss.)* No NFHS-6 national report has been released as of June 2026, so these 2019-21 figures remain the latest nationally representative nutrition data — a vintage problem in itself (see Section 6).

**Workforce and infrastructure.**

10. **Health workforce density is far below the WHO threshold.** Using NSSO 2017-18 and registry data, Karan et al. estimate the density of *active, adequately qualified* doctors at **5.0 per 10,000 people and nurses/midwives at 6.0 per 10,000** — against the WHO threshold of 44.5 doctors-nurses-midwives per 10,000 [10]. The nurse-to-doctor ratio is inverted relative to well-functioning systems.
11. **Specialists are missing where they are needed most.** Rural Health Statistics 2022-23: rural Community Health Centres had 4,413 specialists in place against 21,964 required — a **79.9% shortfall** (surgeons 83.3%, physicians 81.9%, paediatricians 80.5%, obstetrician-gynaecologists 74.2%) [11].
12. **Hospital beds: about 1.3 per 1,000 population overall, and only ~0.79 government beds per 1,000** (Knight Frank–Berkadia estimate, 2023), against the National Health Policy 2017 norm of 2 public beds per 1,000 [33]; the World Bank's last comparable figure for India is 1.6 per 1,000 (2021) [32]. Over 60% of beds are private and concentrated in cities [33].

**Coverage programmes.**

13. **Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY:** independent evidence on its impact is genuinely mixed — see Section 3 for the household survey findings. The scheme offers ₹5 lakh per family per year of cashless hospitalisation cover to over 12 crore poor and vulnerable families (~55 crore people), expanded in 2024 to include all citizens aged 70+; over 42 crore Ayushman cards had been issued by late 2025 [13]. Card issuance is an enrolment metric, not a utilisation or financial-protection metric — multiple independent studies document low actual claim rates and significant coverage gaps alongside the headline numbers.
14. **Ayushman Arogya Mandirs (formerly Health and Wellness Centres): about 1.78 lakh operationalised** as of mid-2025, delivering expanded primary care including NCD screening [14]. Operationalisation, however, is an input measure — staffing, drug stocks and footfall vary widely by state.

## 2. Root causes

**2.1 Public financing is low — and what exists is not fully absorbed.** At 1.43% of GDP (NHA 2022-23) [1a], India's public health spending is among the lowest of major economies relative to income. But money alone is not the binding constraint everywhere: National Health Mission fund utilisation has been chronically incomplete. Unspent NHM balances with states rose to ₹9,509 crore by 2015-16 [18]; NIPFP's analysis documents low absorption in precisely the states with the weakest health outcomes, driven by missing complementary inputs (staff, facilities) and slow fund flows — CAG audits found state treasuries took 50 to 271 days to move released funds to health societies [17][18]. Raising spending without fixing the plumbing produces lapsed budgets, not health.

**2.2 The workforce is too small, wrongly mixed, and badly distributed.** With ~5 qualified active doctors and ~6 nurses/midwives per 10,000 (NSSO 2017-18 basis) [10], India is short by an estimated 1.5-1.8 million workers against the WHO 44.5 threshold [10]. The shortage is most acute for nurses and for specialists in rural facilities (80% CHC specialist shortfall, RHS 2022-23) [11]. More than 20% of qualified professionals are not in the health labour market at all [10] — a retention and posting problem, not just a production problem.

**2.3 Primary care is too weak to manage the NCD transition.** With ~62% of deaths from chronic disease [12], the system needs continuous, protocol-based care close to home: screening, cheap generic drugs, follow-up. Before recent reforms, only a small fraction of India's estimated hypertensive adults were on treatment with controlled blood pressure; the India Hypertension Control Initiative had enrolled over 4 million patients by 2022 — important progress, but ~12.5% of the estimated hypertensive population in covered areas [27][28]. Untreated chronic disease surfaces later as expensive hospitalisation — the most regressive way to buy health.

**2.4 Medicines and diagnostics drive impoverishing payments.** Medicines are the single largest component of household out-of-pocket spending in NHA estimates [1]. Where states have not built reliable free-drug systems, patients buy from private pharmacies at retail prices. Evidence from Uttar Pradesh shows how bad the baseline can be: before its supply-chain redesign on the Tamil Nadu model, overall essential medicine availability across public warehouses was around 34% (December 2020 baseline) [25].

**2.5 Governance and quality are thin: no public health cadres, weak audit.** Tamil Nadu is the only state with a full directorate of public health staffed by a dedicated, trained public health cadre at district level, under a Public Health Act dating to 1939 [23][24]. Most states assign public health functions to clinicians without public health training or career tracks. On the demand-side programmes, the CAG's 2023 performance audit of PM-JAY found ineligible registrations (9.85 lakh beneficiaries linked to a single mobile number), claims paid for patients earlier recorded as dead (₹6.97 crore across 3,903 claims), and ₹57.53 crore of excess payments to providers in four states [16] — evidence that fraud controls and data quality lag the scheme's scale.

**2.6 Dual practice and the unregulated private sector.** The private sector delivers over 70% of outpatient care and holds over 60% of hospital beds [33]. A structural feature this large cannot be treated as background noise. Public-sector workforce expansion is partially offset by dual practice: doctors holding government posts who also run private clinics, which diverts both their time and their patients. Pooled drug procurement lowers costs for the public facility but does not reach the patient buying from a private pharmacy. PM-JAY's documented 'shift toward private facilities' (Section 3) means the scheme is financing private-sector growth rather than substituting it. Any plan that strengthens the public system without acknowledging private-sector lock-in — through trust, convenience, or drug-dispensing income — will find OOPE and NCD control targets harder to reach than the public-supply arithmetic suggests.

**2.7 Federal inequality compounds everything.** Kerala's IMR (5) versus Madhya Pradesh/UP/Chhattisgarh (37) in SRS 2023 [6]; Assam versus Kerala on MMR [7]. The states with the worst outcomes also have the lowest per-capita health spending, weakest absorption and thinnest workforce [17]. A national average is therefore a misleading target; the policy problem is disproportionately an eight-state problem.

## 3. What has worked

### International benchmarks

**Thailand — Universal Coverage Scheme (UCS), 2002.** A tax-financed scheme covering the informal sector, built on capitation payment to primary-care networks and a strong purchaser (NHSO). Measured outcomes: the incidence of catastrophic health spending (OOP >10% of household consumption) fell from about 6% in 1996 to under 3-4% within the first decade of UHC; OOP's share of current health expenditure fell from **34% (2000, pre-UCS) to 11% (2017)** [19]; for the period since UCS launch, government share rose from 65% (2002) to 78% (2016) while OOP fell from 28% to 12% over that same 2002-2016 window [19]. Thailand also closed provincial gaps in child mortality. *Transferability caveat:* Thailand spent decades building district hospitals and a rural health workforce *before* 2002, and is a unitary state of 70 million people; India must do workforce-building and coverage expansion simultaneously, state by state.

**Brazil — Família Saúde / Family Health Strategy (ESF).** Salaried teams (doctor, nurse, 4-6 community health agents) each responsible for a defined catchment of ~3,500 people, delivering proactive primary care within the SUS universal system [20]. Measured outcomes: Aquino et al.'s national municipal panel study (AJPH, 2009) finds FHS expansion associated with falling IMR in 73% of Brazilian municipalities [20]; the effect is strongest on post-neonatal deaths, the deaths primary care can most plausibly prevent [20]. A sub-national case study from Paraná's 3rd health region illustrates the pattern: IMR fell from 17.1 to 10.7 per 1,000 as FHS coverage rose from 44% to 66% between 2005 and 2016 [21] — this regional figure is illustrative, not nationally representative. *These are panel associations with selection issues; the causal estimate is lower-bounded.* *Transferability caveat:* effects are correlational-to-quasi-experimental, strongest where coverage was sustained for years; Brazil pays its CHW agents full salaries, unlike India's honorarium-based ASHAs.

**Sri Lanka (supporting case).** Maternal mortality around 18 per 100,000 and infant mortality near 5 per 1,000 (2022-23, World Bank/WHO data), achieved with total health spending of roughly 3% of GDP, built on free public primary care and a cadre of salaried public health midwives attending 98%+ institutional deliveries [22]. *Caveat:* small country, high female literacy, century-long head start on civil registration. (The CGD case study [22] provides narrative detail; current statistics are from WHO/World Bank global health databases.)

### Indian state-level successes

**Tamil Nadu — public health cadre + pooled drug procurement.** Two institutions matter. First, the Directorate of Public Health, staffed by a dedicated cadre of public-health-trained doctors managing districts under the Tamil Nadu Public Health Act, 1939 [23][24]. Second, the Tamil Nadu Medical Services Corporation (TNMSC, est. 1995): centralised tendering for a stable essential drugs list, district warehouses, passbook-based facility drawing rights, and pre-dispatch quality testing [23][25]. Measured outcomes: TN's IMR (18), U5MR (22) and MMR (60) are all far below national averages of the same vintage (NFHS-5 2019-21 / SRS 2016-18: 35, 42, 113) [24]. *Caveat:* TN's results reflect 80+ years of institutional accumulation and high female literacy; the cadre and the corporation are replicable, the head start is not. *State selection in this brief reflects the availability of independently published outcome studies, not political affiliation. States with different political alignments also show above-average performance on specific metrics — Himachal Pradesh on immunisation coverage, Odisha on Biju Swasthya Kalyan Yojana enrolment — but the depth of independently evaluated institutional evidence is strongest for TN and Kerala.*

**Uttar Pradesh supply-chain redesign (TNMSC model).** When UP redesigned its medicines supply chain on the TNMSC model (incorporating UPMSC in 2019), overall availability of essential medicines across all warehouses improved from **34% to 88%** (December 2020 to July 2024); average availability per warehouse improved from **27% to 97%** over the same period, with an average of 275 of 287 listed medicines in stock per warehouse by mid-2024 [25]. These are warehouse-level figures; the paper does not separately report facility-level (PHC) availability after redesign, so the last-mile picture is not fully captured. *Caveat:* this is a supply-chain programme evaluation, not a randomised study; selection effects and parallel improvements cannot be ruled out.

**Chhattisgarh — Mitanin community health workers (2002).** Nearly 70,000 women CHWs covering virtually all rural hamlets, selected by communities, supported by a dedicated technical body (the State Health Resource Centre) — the explicit prototype for the national ASHA programme [26][37]. Measured outcomes: a quasi-experimental evaluation estimated annual average reductions of approximately **4–5% in underweight and 5–6% in stunting** in programme areas (Vir et al. 2014, Food and Nutrition Bulletin, as cited in Nandi & Schneider [26]); qualitative evaluations document effective community action on social determinants (nutrition entitlements, gender violence) [26][37]. *Caveat:* not an RCT; causal attribution is uncertain; effects depend on the SHRC-style support structure and sustained training, which several states copying the ASHA form did not copy in substance. No confidence interval was reported in the cited evaluation.

**Kerala — Aardram mission Family Health Centres (2016- ).** Upgrading PHCs into Family Health Centres with extended evening hours, more staff, and expanded NCD and mental-health screening. A 2024 facility survey found FHCs delivered higher annual outpatient utilisation (11,343 vs 9,580 visits per 10,000 population in comparable PHCs) and offered services — depression and COPD screening — that ordinary PHCs did not [29]. *Caveat:* early-stage evidence, facility-level not population-level outcomes; Kerala's baseline human development is exceptional.

**On PM-JAY itself:** the best multi-state household evidence (57,000+ scheme-eligible individuals in six states) finds PM-JAY associated with a **13% relative reduction in out-of-pocket spending and a 21% relative reduction in catastrophic health expenditure**, with no increase in overall hospitalisation rates but a shift toward private facilities [30]. The paper [30] reports these as relative changes; absolute baseline and absolute savings figures are not separately reported in the published study, so the magnitude in rupee terms cannot be directly assessed from this citation alone. Other studies find weaker or no financial protection and document exclusion of the poorest from enrolment (see Section 6). The honest read: demand-side insurance has helped, is not sufficient, and depends on supply-side strength it cannot itself create.

## 4. The 5-year plan (FY2027-FY2031)

Six interventions. Each follows the same logic: fix the constraint the evidence identifies, through the level of government that actually controls it.

### 4.1 Raise public health spending to 2.5% of GDP — tied to absorption reform

- **Problem:** GHE at 1.43% of GDP (NHA 2022-23) [1a]; the NHP 2017 target of 2.5% by 2025 has been missed [4]. Simultaneously, NHM funds go unspent in weak states (treasury delays of 50-271 days documented by CAG) [17][18]. The most recent documented NHM utilisation figures are from NIPFP [17] and IndiaSpend [18] covering up to FY15-16; more recent state-level utilisation data is not uniformly published. Accountability Initiative's PAISA dashboards and state budget documents suggest continued absorption gaps in EAG states, but a precise all-India FY24 figure is not available from a citable published source.
- **Intervention:** A centre-state compact: the centre raises NHM and PM-ABHIM transfers on a published glide path; states qualify for the increment by (a) transferring funds from treasury to implementing societies within 15 days, (b) filling sanctioned posts above a threshold, (c) publishing quarterly utilisation. Increments front-loaded to the eight Empowered Action Group states.
- **Mechanism:** Conditional grants change state behaviour where unconditional ones lapse; the binding constraint in poor states is absorption capacity, so the money must purchase capacity (staff, systems) first [17].
- **Target & baseline:** Government health expenditure from ~1.9% of GDP (centre+state BE 2024-25) [4] to **2.5% of GDP by FY2031**; NHM utilisation ≥85% in every major state (baseline documented at ~71% in FY15-16 across surveyed states [17][18]; more recent data unavailable from a published source).
- **Who implements:** Union Ministry of Finance + MoHFW (transfers, conditions); state finance and health departments (absorption); Fifteenth/Sixteenth Finance Commission architecture for health grants.
- **Indicative cost:** This *is* the overall cost envelope — see Section 5. (+0.6% of GDP/year at maturity ≈ ₹1.9-2 lakh crore at FY25 GDP of ₹330.68 lakh crore [35].)
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Conditionality can punish exactly the states whose citizens most need spending; mitigate with capacity-building grants rather than pure penalties. Nominal targets as %-of-GDP slip silently if GDP grows fast and budgets don't.

### 4.2 Dedicated public health cadres and Public Health Acts in every major state

- **Problem:** Only Tamil Nadu runs district health through a trained public health cadre under a Public Health Act [23][24]; elsewhere clinicians manage epidemics, sanitation and programmes without public health training or career incentives.
- **Intervention:** Each state notifies a three-tier public health management cadre (block–district–state), with MPH-qualified entry, separate from the clinical cadre, plus a model State Public Health Act (drafts already exist with MoHFW/NITI).
- **Mechanism:** Tamil Nadu's outcome edge is credibly attributed in the health-systems literature to managerial specialisation: people whose entire career ladder is population health, not surgery interrupted by administration [23][24].
- **Target & baseline:** From 1 state with a full cadre (baseline 2026) to **all 20 large states notified and ≥60% of district public health officer posts filled by trained candidates by FY2031**.
- **Who implements:** State governments (cadre rules are a state subject); MoHFW provides model rules and NHM funds the transition; public health schools (IIPH network, medical colleges) supply training.
- **Indicative cost:** Modest — salary differential and training for roughly 2-3 senior officers per district plus block officers; the relevant comparator is administrative cost within the existing NHM envelope (Tamil Nadu runs its directorate within an ordinary state health budget [24]). Reliable all-India costing unavailable; budget line should be built per state.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Cadre proposals have failed repeatedly across large states over 20+ years — Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Rajasthan have all seen proposals blocked or quietly shelved. The resistance is not only about post-design: IAS and clinical-cadre lobbies resist delegated authority, and health departments are frequently captured by clinical associations with political leverage. Phased entry (new posts, not conversions) reduces direct conflict but does not resolve budget competition or career-prestige dynamics. A realistic mitigation is parliamentary or state legislative backing for cadre rules rather than administrative orders, which can be reversed without political cost. A cadre without delegated financial powers is a nameplate regardless of how it is established.

### 4.3 Free essential medicines and diagnostics everywhere, via TNMSC-style pooled procurement

- **Problem:** Medicines are the largest component of out-of-pocket spending [1]; availability in weak-state public warehouses has been as low as 27–34% (UP pre-reform baseline, 2020) [25].
- **Intervention:** Every state operates an autonomous procurement corporation on the TNMSC design (stable EDL, e-tendered rate contracts, district warehouses, facility passbooks, NABL-tested batches, real-time stock IT) [23][25]; centre co-finances through the existing NHM Free Drugs & Diagnostics Service Initiatives and makes publication of monthly facility-level stock-out data a condition.
- **Mechanism:** Pooled procurement cuts unit prices and corruption simultaneously (single transparent tender vs thousands of local purchases); guaranteed supply is what moves patients from private pharmacies back to free public care. UP's redesign raised overall warehouse availability from 34% to 88%, and average per-warehouse availability from 27% to 97% (2020-2024) [25]; note that warehouse availability is a necessary but not sufficient condition — drugs must still reach PHC level. Rajasthan's Mukhyamantri Nishulk Dava Yojana supplies 1,795 medicines/surgicals free statewide [34].
- **Target & baseline:** ≥90% availability of essential-list medicines in public facilities in every state by FY2030 (baseline: 27–34% warehouse-level in pre-reform UP [25]); OOPE share of THE below **30%** by FY2031 (baseline 43.4%, NHA 2022-23 [1a]). *The arithmetic: medicines are the largest OOPE component but not the only one. Eliminating medicine costs from public-facility patients would reduce total OOPE by considerably less than the full gap to 30% — the target also requires parallel action on diagnostics, user fees, and private-sector price regulation, which are described in Section 4.3 but should be understood as jointly necessary, not individually sufficient.*
- **Who implements:** State health departments via procurement corporations; MoHFW (NHM) co-financing; state drug controllers for quality.
- **Indicative cost:** Rajasthan's experience is the best cost basis: a comprehensive state free-drug scheme is centrally co-financed at up to 60% under NHM [34]. TNMSC-type systems historically deliver drugs at a fraction of retail prices [23][25], so the gross cost is partly offset by household savings.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Warehouse availability (88–97% post-reform in UP [25]) does not guarantee last-mile delivery to PHCs — the last-mile gap is where patients are actually lost to private pharmacies. Stock-outs from poor demand forecasting (documented even in TN [25]); corporations need professional staffing and political insulation from local-purchase lobbies. Free drugs without prescribing audits can fuel irrational prescription.

### 4.4 A primary-care chronic disease platform: scale IHCI-style protocol care through 1.78 lakh Ayushman Arogya Mandirs

- **Problem:** NCDs cause ~62% of deaths [12], but only a fraction of hypertensives/diabetics are diagnosed, treated and controlled; anaemia among women is 57% (NFHS-5, 2019-21) [9].
- **Intervention:** Universalise the India Hypertension Control Initiative package — simple drug protocols, guaranteed drug supply at the AAM, task-sharing with nurses/CHOs, a digital treatment register with cohort-based reporting — across all districts, extended to diabetes; link to annual population screening of adults 30+ already mandated under NP-NCD.
- **Mechanism:** IHCI is the rare Indian NCD intervention with measured results at scale: rolled out with WHO support to 130+ districts, enrolling 4+ million patients by 2022 [27][28]. Programme cohort data reported by WHO and Resolve to Save Lives — both IHCI partners — show **blood pressure control rising from 37% to 48% among patients enrolled in programme facilities, and the share of patients paying out of pocket for BP drugs falling from 47% to 9%** [27][28]. These figures come from programme-internal cohort tracking, not an independent controlled comparison; treatment intensification and cohort regression-to-the-mean effects cannot be fully excluded. Independent replication at scale has not been published; treat these as promising internal evidence, not a confirmed effect size. The mechanism is nonetheless coherent and proven elsewhere: protocols + assured drugs + follow-up registers.
- **Target & baseline:** BP control ≥60% among patients under treatment in public facilities by FY2031 (baseline 48%, IHCI cohorts [28]); all 1.78 lakh AAMs [14] reporting monthly NCD cohort data.
- **Who implements:** MoHFW (NP-NCD, NHM) sets protocol and finances drugs; states run it through AAM staff (Community Health Officers, ANMs); WHO-India/ICMR for technical monitoring.
- **Indicative cost:** WHO describes IHCI as a "high impact, low-cost" intervention — its costs are dominated by generic drugs (amlodipine, telmisartan-class) procured at pooled-tender prices [27]; the delivery infrastructure (AAMs, CHOs) is already financed under NHM/PM-ABHIM [5][15].
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Screening without treatment capacity creates worried-well and wasted data; sequence drugs-first. Digital registers can become data-entry burdens that crowd out care; keep indicators few.

### 4.5 Fix the secondary tier: district hospitals, specialists, and a nursing surge

- **Problem:** 80% specialist shortfall at rural CHCs (RHS 2022-23) [11]; 0.79 public beds per 1,000 [33]; nurse density (~6/10,000 active qualified) below doctor-appropriate skill mix [10]. Neonatal mortality (19/1,000, SRS 2021 [8]) is now the hard core of child deaths and is a facility-quality problem.
- **Intervention:** (a) Complete PM-ABHIM's 602 critical-care blocks and 730 district integrated public health labs on schedule [15]; (b) operate the 157 new co-located government nursing colleges (Cabinet-approved 2023, ₹1,570 crore, ~15,700 additional graduates/year) at full capacity and add a second tranche [31]; (c) attack the specialist gap with bonded district-service postgraduate seats in district hospitals (DNB programmes) and team-based task-sharing (e.g., anaesthesia assistants, obstetric nurse practitioners) rather than waiting for specialists who will not come.
- **Mechanism:** Thailand and Sri Lanka both built outcomes on functional district hospitals staffed heavily by nurses and midwives, not on specialist density [19][22]; India's own neonatal and maternal mortality residue is concentrated where CEmONC-capable facilities are missing — Tamil Nadu's maternal-death decline tracked its build-out of comprehensive emergency obstetric care centres [24].
- **Target & baseline:** Halve the CHC specialist shortfall to ≤40% by FY2031 (baseline 79.9%, RHS 2022-23 [11]); government beds from 0.79 to ≥1.2 per 1,000 [33]; +75,000 cumulative additional nursing graduates by FY2031 (basis: 15,700/yr from the 157 colleges [31]).
- **Who implements:** Centre: MoHFW (PM-ABHIM, nursing colleges, DNB seats via NBE). States: recruitment, hospital operations. District: hospital management societies.
- **Indicative cost:** PM-ABHIM's existing envelope is ₹64,180 crore (2021-26) [15]; nursing expansion cost basis ₹10 crore/college [31]. A successor PM-ABHIM-II of comparable scale is the realistic vehicle.
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Buildings without staff — PM-ABHIM's own risk — argue for tying capital releases to staffing plans. Bonded rural service has a mixed record: evasion rates in prior state schemes have ranged from 30–60%, and enforcement actions (bar from further registration, degree withholding) have faced legal challenges. The target of halving the specialist shortfall is achievable only with simultaneous improvements in facility living conditions, spousal employment opportunities, and school access in posting areas. Without these non-financial conditions, the bottleneck shifts from training supply to placement compliance — and the five-year target slips regardless of how many seats are sanctioned.

### 4.6 Measure quality and stop the leaks: death audits, IPHS dashboards, PM-JAY scheme integrity

This intervention has two distinct sub-goals with different rationales.

**Sub-goal (a): Quality measurement.** The system measures inputs, not outcomes; facility quality against Indian Public Health Standards is largely unreported. Intervention: mandatory confidential maternal/neonatal/in-hospital death audits in every district hospital, published annually in aggregate; a public IPHS-compliance dashboard for every PHC/CHC/DH (the IPHS 2022 framework and NQAS certification machinery already exist). Mechanism: Tamil Nadu and Kerala's improvements were measurement-led (TN's maternal death audits are central to its MMR record [24]); transparency changes manager behaviour at near-zero marginal cost.

**Sub-goal (b): Scheme integrity.** PM-JAY's fiscal legitimacy and equity depend on fraud controls working. The CAG found PM-JAY paying claims for patients recorded dead, lakhs of beneficiaries on single phone numbers, and ₹57.53 crore in excess provider payments [16]. Fraud money does not reach the intended poor; it either exits the system or subsidises ineligible users. Intervention: implement the CAG's PM-JAY recommendations — Aadhaar-validated beneficiary cleanup, real-time duplicate-admission flags, recovery of irregular payments, and independent state anti-fraud units [16]. Insurance schemes worldwide leak 3-10% to fraud absent active controls — the CAG findings show India is not exempt [16].

- **Problem:** See above.
- **Intervention:** See above.
- **Mechanism:** See above.
- **Target & baseline:** *Quality:* 100% of district hospitals conducting published death audits by FY2029 (baseline: no national publication); ≥50% of public facilities NQAS-assessed by FY2031. *Integrity:* PM-JAY: zero claims payable against deceased-patient IDs in audit re-tests (baseline: 3,903 such claims paid, CAG 2023 [16]).
- **Who implements:** National Health Authority (PM-JAY controls); MoHFW + NHSRC (IPHS/NQAS); state quality assurance committees; CAG follow-up audits.
- **Indicative cost:** Small — audit cells and dashboards are administrative costs within NHM programme management (capped at a low single-digit share of NHM budgets); the CAG recoveries identified already exceed plausible system costs [16].
- **Risks & trade-offs:** Punitive use of audit data drives under-reporting; the international norm is confidential, no-blame clinical audit with public aggregate reporting. Dashboards can be gamed; rotate independent verification.

## 5. What it costs overall

- **Where spending is now:** Government health expenditure was **1.43% of GDP in 2022-23** (NHA 2022-23) [1a], down from the COVID-peak of 1.84% in FY22; budgeted centre+state spending ran near **1.9% of GDP in 2024-25** (PRS) [4]. The centre's health ministry budget for FY2026-27 is **₹1,06,530 crore** [5]; states together account for the larger share of public health spending [1][4].
- **The plan's headline number:** reaching the National Health Policy target of **2.5% of GDP by FY2031** means adding roughly **0.6 percentage points of GDP per year at maturity**. At FY2024-25 nominal GDP of **₹330.68 lakh crore** (NSO Provisional Estimates, May 2025) [35], 0.6% ≈ **₹1.9-2.0 lakh crore per year** in today's terms, phased in over five years and split between centre and states roughly along existing NHM/finance-commission lines. This envelope *contains* interventions 4.2-4.6: the cadres, drug corporations, NCD platform, PM-ABHIM-II (current PM-ABHIM: ₹64,180 crore over five years [15]) and nursing expansion (₹1,570 crore for 157 colleges [31]) are all claims on it, not additions to it.
- **What the money buys back:** every percentage point shifted from out-of-pocket to public financing is regressive household spending avoided; NHA 2022-23 confirms OOPE has risen back to 43.4% post-pandemic [1a], underscoring that public spending retreats are not costless. Thailand bought catastrophic-spending incidence below 3-4% with a government share of 78% of health spending [19] — India at 2.5% of GDP would still be financing a leaner system than that.
- **Honest comparison:** the increment (~₹2 lakh crore/year at maturity) is roughly twice the centre's entire current annual health budget [5]. It is achievable only if states — who must hire the nurses and run the hospitals — raise their own health allocations. What this requires is large: for the eight Empowered Action Group states, reaching 2.5% of state GDP in health would require health to grow from its current approximate 4–5% of state budgets to roughly 7–8%. This is a demanding ask but comparable to what Tamil Nadu and Kerala already spend. State-wise fiscal data on health budget shares are available from NIPFP and Accountability Initiative; the plan's feasibility should be stress-tested against the weakest-capacity states, not the average.

## 6. What we don't know / where we could be wrong

1. **Our nutrition and household-health data are stale.** NFHS-5 fieldwork ended in 2021. As of June 2026 no NFHS-6 national report is available, so anaemia, stunting and service-coverage figures here are five-plus years old [9]. If post-COVID recovery (or deterioration) has been large, parts of Section 1 are wrong in unknown directions.
2. **The OOPE number is contested.** NHA 2021-22's 39.4% [1] reflects a pandemic year with exceptional public spending; peer-reviewed syntheses report OOPE estimates in the 47-49% range for nearby years [30]. We use the official series but flag that the true current share may be several points higher.
3. **PM-JAY's impact evidence cuts both ways.** A six-state household study finds 13%/21% relative reductions in OOPE/catastrophic spending [30]; other peer-reviewed work finds publicly funded insurance failing to protect the poorest, and exclusion errors in enrolment. Our plan treats demand-side insurance as necessary-but-insufficient; if the optimistic estimates are right, supply-side spending could show lower marginal returns than we assume — and vice versa.
4. **Anaemia measurement itself is disputed.** The NFHS capillary-blood method likely overstates anaemia relative to venous samples (a known debate around the Diet and Biomarkers Survey); the 57%/67% figures [9] may be biased upward, though the adverse *trend* between NFHS-4 and NFHS-5 is harder to dismiss.
5. **State success stories may not transfer.** Tamil Nadu and Kerala built on decades of literacy, female agency and administrative depth [23][24]; the Mitanin estimates are quasi-experimental, not randomised [26]; Brazil's ESF effects are strongest in municipal panel correlations [20][21]. We have weighted institutional designs (cadres, procurement corporations, CHW support structures) that have a plausible mechanism *and* replication evidence (UP's supply chain [25]), but replication failure is a live risk.
6. **Absorption is the plan's biggest internal tension.** We propose more money (4.1) while citing evidence that weak states cannot spend current allocations [17][18]. The conditional-grant design is our answer, but conditional financing in Indian federalism has a mixed record. The counter-argument — that governance reform should *precede* any spending increase — is the strongest objection to this brief. Bihar and UP have seen parallel-sequencing tried before: NHM releases in both states produced large unspent balances documented in CAG performance audits (notably FY2011-16 in Bihar; FY2014-18 in UP), precisely because complementary inputs — posts, facilities, trained staff — were missing. We judge sequencing them together to be right because those same complementary inputs require money to build; without the funding, the governance reforms have no material to work with. But the earlier failures used unconditional grants; this plan's conditional-grant architecture is different in design, and we should be clear that the mechanism is untested at this scale in India. Observable evidence within 24 months that would cause us to revise toward sequencing governance first: if any two large EAG states show NHM utilisation still below 60% after conditional grants have been in place for two full fiscal years, the conditionality is not working and the sequencing argument needs to be revisited.
7. **We assume meaningful patient shift from private to public care as public facilities improve.** If private-sector lock-in — through brand trust, convenience, or drug-dispensing income — is stronger than supply-side improvements imply, OOPE and NCD control targets will not be reached by the mechanisms described. The plan has no private-sector regulation or accreditation component; that is a deliberate scope boundary, but readers should weigh it. The dominant structural uncertainty in Indian health policy is whether a strengthened public system attracts patients away from private care, or whether the private sector retains them regardless.
8. **Numbers we deliberately did not use:** state-wise MMR levels from the 2020-22 SRS bulletin (we could not independently parse the bulletin PDF, so we cite only the all-India figure and the direction of state gaps [7]); cumulative PM-JAY admission counts (official communications in 2025 ranged widely across releases); and any costing of state public health cadres (no reliable all-India estimate exists — "reliable recent data unavailable").
